# Tort Categories in Nursing

**Topic:** 1 — Management of Care &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Pattern:** Dimensions &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Tags:** 1.14, IP-CD, state-variance
**Status:** draft (SME review pending)

The seven tort categories most often tested under NCLEX 1.14 (Legal Rights and Responsibilities). Tort law is US common law — the operative definitions, defenses, and elements come from each state's case law and Restatement adoption rather than from a federal statute. Cornell Legal Information Institute (LII) is the primary public reference for the common-law elements; StatPearls and the open NCBI Bookshelf nursing-management chapters supply the nursing-practice examples. The federal floor that does exist (HIPAA for invasion of privacy, EMTALA for some negligence framings) is noted in cells when it materially shapes the duty. The `state-variance` Tag applies because the operative elements and defenses are state common law; the unsourced-values table flags every cell where the value reflects predominant US convention rather than a single primary citation.

## Notes

- **Cell convention:** each `<br>`-separated item is one atomic Fact. No parentheticals in cells.
- **Why this Concept carries `state-variance`.** Tort law in the US is state common law. There is no federal tort code that defines negligence, battery, or false imprisonment for civil litigation; states adopt the Restatement (Second) or (Third) of Torts to varying degrees and develop their own case law. The four-element malpractice framework (duty / breach / causation / damages) and the elements listed in this Concept are the predominant US convention drawn from Cornell LII and the Restatement tradition, but a given state may add or refine elements (e.g., some states require gross negligence for certain healthcare contexts; some recognize all four Prosser invasion-of-privacy categories, others recognize only some). Tag the cells that depend on convention rather than a single statute in the unsourced-values table below.
- **Negligence vs. malpractice.** Both are unintentional torts; malpractice is the subset of negligence committed by a licensed professional acting in their professional capacity, judged against the professional standard of care rather than the ordinary-reasonable-person standard [s2, s3]. The five-element negligence framing (Cornell LII) and the four-element malpractice framing (StatPearls and PMC) reflect the same tort with two different framings — the LII negligence list separates cause-in-fact from proximate cause, while the StatPearls malpractice list folds them into a single "causation" element. Both are correct; the Concept presents each as the source presents it so reviewers can verify cell-by-cell.
- **Assault and battery in nursing are separate intentional torts.** Many criminal codes merge them, but in civil tort litigation they remain distinct: assault is the apprehension, battery is the contact [s5, s7]. A nurse who threatens to restrain a refusing client (assault) and then physically restrains them without authority (battery + false imprisonment) commits all three torts simultaneously.
- **Battery in nursing turns on consent.** A competent client's refusal of any touching — even a beneficial touching like an injection — makes the touching a battery if performed [s2, s7]. The exception is the implied-consent doctrine in emergencies where the client cannot consent and a reasonable person would consent [s2]. This is the same doctrine that cross-references `consent-edge-cases.md` (sibling Concept).
- **False imprisonment and restraints.** Restraints applied per a valid provider order and per agency policy under 42 CFR 482.13(e) are lawfully authorized and not false imprisonment [s2]. Restraints applied without an order, beyond the order's duration, or without documented behavioral emergency are false imprisonment [s2]. Sibling Concept `restraint-use-rules.md` covers the order-and-monitoring framework; this Concept covers the tort exposure when those rules are violated.
- **Defamation and the medical record.** Documenting false information in the chart is libel [s2]; verbally telling colleagues false damaging information about a client or coworker is slander [s2]. Truth is an absolute defense to both [s2, s9]. Qualified privilege protects good-faith reports made through proper channels (chain of command, BON complaints, mandatory-reporter notifications) [s2] — this is the same privilege that lets `mandatory-reporting-categories.md` exist without exposing nurses to defamation suits for accurate reports.
- **Invasion of privacy and HIPAA.** HIPAA is the federal floor for the public-disclosure-of-private-facts category in healthcare [s2]; the four Prosser categories in the cell are the underlying tort framework [s10]. A HIPAA violation is not automatically tort liability, but the same conduct often supports both an OCR HIPAA enforcement action and a state-law invasion-of-privacy claim. Sibling Concept `hipaa-disclosure-rules.md` covers the federal disclosure rules; this Concept covers the tort exposure.
- **Cross-Concept links.** Sibling `mandatory-reporting-categories.md` covers when reporting is required and shows why qualified privilege protects the reporter from defamation. Sibling `consent-edge-cases.md` covers who can consent; absence of valid consent is what converts a touching into battery and a procedure into malpractice. Sibling `restraint-use-rules.md` covers the order/monitoring framework that, if violated, becomes false imprisonment. Sibling `hipaa-disclosure-rules.md` covers the privacy-rule federal floor underneath the invasion-of-privacy tort. Out of scope here: criminal assault and battery (covered by state criminal codes, not the civil tort framework), products-liability strict-liability torts, and intentional infliction of emotional distress.
- **Tag note.** Carrying `IP-CD` (Communication and Documentation) because most NCLEX-tested nursing tort exposure runs through documentation acts (charting falsely → libel; verbal report → slander; consent documentation → battery defense; restraint documentation → false-imprisonment defense). `IP-CG` and `IP-CJ` are also defensible; chose `IP-CD` because the exam item-writing predominantly probes the documentation surface. `state-variance` Tag added per the Tag-flexibility convention in the knowledge map — the registry already lists this Concept as a likely future user.

### Tricky distractors

- **Assault vs. battery.** Threatening contact = assault; making contact = battery. Wrong-answer pattern: calling a verbal threat "battery" because no physical line was crossed. The threat alone is the assault; the touching is the battery [s5, s7].
- **Battery vs. negligence.** A nurse who gives the wrong medication by accident is negligent (or malpracticing) — there was no intent to cause offensive contact. A nurse who gives a medication a competent client refused is committing battery — the contact was intentional and unconsented [s2, s7]. The intent-to-contact distinguishes the intentional tort from the unintentional one.
- **False imprisonment requires bounded area.** Telling a competent client "you can't leave AMA without signing the form" is not false imprisonment — the client has reasonable means of safe escape [s8]. Physically blocking the door, locking the unit, or restraining the client in bed without authority is false imprisonment [s2, s8].
- **Defamation requires falsity.** Truthful documentation of a client's behavior, even if unflattering, is not defamation — truth is an absolute defense [s2, s9]. Wrong-answer pattern: "the nurse can be sued for defamation for documenting that the client was hostile to staff." Wrong if the documentation is accurate.
- **Invasion of privacy is broader than HIPAA.** HIPAA enforcement is administrative (OCR fines); the tort is private litigation for damages [s2, s10]. A nurse who accesses a celebrity's chart without a treatment reason can face HIPAA penalties AND a state-law intrusion-upon-seclusion suit. Wrong-answer pattern: assuming "HIPAA covers it" means no civil exposure.
- **Negligence vs. malpractice distinction.** A nurse who slips on a wet floor and drops a sandwich on a visitor is negligent in their personal capacity; the same nurse who fails to assess a patient per professional standards is committing malpractice [s2, s3]. The professional standard of care, not the ordinary-reasonable-person standard, governs malpractice claims.
- **Consent as defense scope.** Consent defends battery and assault, not negligence or malpractice [s2, s7]. A client who consents to a procedure has not waived the right to a non-negligent procedure; a botched procedure is still malpractice even with valid consent.
