# Ethical Principles in Nursing

**Topic:** 1 — Management of Care &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Pattern:** Dimensions &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Tags:** 1.11, IP-CG
**Status:** draft (SME review pending)

The six ethical principles most often tested under NCSBN §1.11 Ethical Practice. Rows are the principles; columns are the shared attributes — definition (what the principle obligates), common dilemma (the principle it most often conflicts with in practice), example violation (a concrete failure that shows up in NCLEX item stems). Sibling Concept `ethical-decision-making-process.md` covers the seven-step process the nurse uses to resolve dilemmas; this Concept stays focused on the principles themselves. NCSBN §1.11 activity statements direct the candidate to "Practice in a manner consistent with the nurses' code of ethics" and to "Recognize and report ethical dilemmas" `[s4]` — recognition requires being able to name the principle in tension, which is exactly what this Concept indexes.

|---|---|---|---|
| Autonomy | Patient's right to make own decisions based on personal values `[s1]`<br>Capacity to determine one's own actions through independent choice `[s2, s3]`<br>Right to refuse treatment `[s1]` | Autonomy vs Beneficence `[s3]`<br>Patient refuses an intervention the nurse believes will help `[s3]` | Withholding diagnosis information to "protect" the patient `[s2]`<br>Making decisions for a competent adult without consent `[s3]`<br>Ignoring an advance directive `[s3]` |
| Beneficence | Duty to act for the patient's benefit `[s2, s3]`<br>Prevent harm `[s2, s3]`<br>Remove harmful conditions `[s2, s3]` | Beneficence vs Autonomy `[s2, s3]`<br>Beneficence vs Justice during resource scarcity `[s3]` | Failing to advocate for vulnerable populations `[s3]`<br>Neglecting to provide comfort to a dying client `[s3]`<br>Withholding timely medication `[s1]` |

## Notes

- **Cell convention:** each `<br>`-separated item is one atomic Fact. No parentheticals in cells.
- **Synthesis-by-direct-derivation disclosure.** "Common dilemma" cells name the principle most often paired against the row's principle in the source literature. The sources state these tensions explicitly (e.g., NBK610435 names "Autonomy vs Beneficence", "Beneficence vs Justice", "Nonmaleficence vs Autonomy", "Fidelity vs Beneficence", "Veracity vs Beneficence" as paired conflicts `[s3]`). The "vs"-pair phrasing is the conventional pedagogical compression of those passages; this is mechanical synthesis, not invention. Cells cite the source(s) that articulate the conflict.
- **Cross-Concept link — `ethical-decision-making-process.md`.** The seven-step process Concept covers how the nurse navigates a dilemma between any two principles named here. Step 5 (Evaluate options) is where these principles are applied. This Concept indexes the principles; the sibling indexes the procedure.
- **ANA Code of Ethics provisioning.** All three NCBI Bookshelf chapters reference the ANA Code of Ethics for Nurses with Interpretive Statements as the canonical professional articulation of these principles. NCSBN §1.11 directs candidates to "Practice in a manner consistent with the nurses' code of ethics" `[s4]`. Specific provision numbers are not modeled here — they belong in a future `ana-code-of-ethics-provisions.md` Concept if SME wants depth.
- **Why six rows, not seven or more.** Accountability appears in `[s1]` as a principle but is widely treated as a professional obligation (Provision 4 of the ANA Code) rather than a discrete principle paired against the others in dilemma analysis. NCLEX items frame the dilemma-recognition skill around the six principles in the table. Veracity and Fidelity are sometimes folded into Autonomy (informed-consent literature) but are listed here as separate rows because NCSBN-aligned secondary sources `[s2, s3]` list them separately and the engine benefits from the additional Rows for distractor sourcing.
- **Out of scope.** ANA Code of Ethics provision numbers, the historical Belmont Report origin of these principles, and bioethics-committee composition are not modeled in this Concept.

### Tricky distractors

- **Beneficence vs Nonmaleficence.** Both serve the patient's welfare but from opposite directions: beneficence acts *to benefit*, nonmaleficence acts *to avoid harm*. Wrong-answer pattern: collapsing them into "do good." In a question stem where treatment itself causes suffering, the conflict is nonmaleficence (avoid the suffering) vs beneficence (provide the treatment) — picking either label alone misses the conflict.
- **Autonomy vs Beneficence.** The single most-tested pairing. Wrong-answer pattern: choosing "beneficence" when the stem describes a competent patient refusing treatment. The principle being upheld in respecting refusal is autonomy; the principle being subordinated is beneficence.
- **Fidelity vs Veracity.** Both involve trust, but fidelity is *keeping promises* and veracity is *telling the truth*. Wrong-answer pattern: labeling a missed-promise scenario as a veracity violation. The nurse who said "I'll be back in an hour" and didn't return broke fidelity, not veracity (the statement was honest at the time).
- **Justice as equality vs equity.** Sources frame justice as both equality (same care) and equity (fair distribution) `[s2, s3]`. Wrong-answer pattern: assuming justice always means identical treatment. Equity-framed scarcity allocation (e.g., transplant lists prioritizing greatest medical need) is justice, not a violation of it.
- **Autonomy with diminished capacity.** Autonomy applies to *competent* adults. Wrong-answer pattern: invoking autonomy to justify ignoring safety interventions for a confused or sedated patient. Capacity is a precondition; absent capacity, surrogate decision-making applies.
