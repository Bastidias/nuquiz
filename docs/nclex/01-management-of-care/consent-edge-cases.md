# Consent Edge Cases

**Topic:** 1 — Management of Care &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Pattern:** Dimensions &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Tags:** 1.12, 1.14, IP-CS
**Status:** draft (SME review pending)

The six populations whose informed-consent pathway departs from the normal-case rule (competent adult signs for self). Sibling Concept `informed-consent-roles.md` covers the normal case; this Concept covers the exceptions. Rows are populations; columns capture who legally consents, the federal-floor exception condition that triggers the alternate pathway, and what must be charted. The federal floor here is 42 CFR 482.13 (CMS Conditions of Participation — patient rights, advance directives, restraint and seclusion) and 42 CFR 482.51(b)(2) (informed consent for surgical services, except in emergencies). State law sets the specifics for who counts as a minor's surrogate, who may be deemed emancipated, what triggers a mental-health hold, and which surrogate-decision-maker hierarchy applies for a cognitively impaired adult. Cells where the federal floor is silent and state law governs are flagged in the unsourced-values table.

## Notes

- **Cell convention:** each `<br>`-separated item is one atomic Fact. No parentheticals in cells.
- **Federal floor vs state variation.** 42 CFR 482.13(b)(2) `[s2]` repeatedly defers to "State law" for who qualifies as the patient's representative — for minors, emancipated minors, cognitively impaired adults, and mental-health holds, the specifics (age cutoffs, surrogate hierarchy, emancipation criteria, involuntary-commitment thresholds) are state-set. Cells driven by state-specific rules are flagged `[needs source]` and listed below; this Concept does not enumerate state-by-state variants.
- **Why "Mental-health hold" looks different from the others.** The other five Rows describe alternate pathways for a *single* consent decision. Mental-health hold is a continuing-care state, and the federally-floored content shifts from the consent form to restraint/seclusion governance under 42 CFR 482.13(e) `[s2]` — order requirements, time limits, face-to-face evaluation, monitoring, documentation. Routine treatment refusals and court-ordered medication during a hold are governed by state mental-health statute, which varies; those land in the unsourced-values table.
- **Emergency-doctrine framing.** 42 CFR 482.51(b)(2) `[s3]` requires a properly executed informed-consent form before surgery "except in emergencies." The cell phrase "implied consent" is a synthesis-by-direct-derivation of the regulation's emergency exception — the regulation defines the exception condition; the long-standing common-law label for what fills the consent gap is "implied consent." Cited to `[s3]` with this Notes disclosure.
- **Source caveat about 45 CFR 46.** 45 CFR 46.116 `[s4]` and 45 CFR 46.408 `[s5]` are the Common Rule (research-subject protections), not direct clinical-consent regulation. They are cited here for two principles that the federal clinical floor borrows from: (a) consent information must be in language understandable to the subject — applied to the Non-English speaker Row; (b) when children are research subjects, IRBs solicit child *assent* alongside parental *permission* — applied as the Minor Row's "assent when capable" cell. SME may prefer to replace `[s5]`'s assent citation with a clinical pediatric-ethics source if one is available.
- **Cross-Concept links.** Sibling `informed-consent-roles.md` (1.12) covers the normal case (provider-RN-client-rep-interpreter roles). Sibling `restraint-use-rules.md` (1.5) covers restraint and seclusion in depth, including the type-by-monitoring-frequency-by-max-duration matrix; this Concept's Mental-health hold Row references those rules but does not duplicate them. Sibling `advance-directive-types.md` (1.1) covers the document types whose existence determines who consents for a cognitively impaired patient.
- **Out of scope.** State-by-state minor age-of-majority cutoffs (typically 18, but lower in a few states for specific decisions). State-by-state emancipation criteria. State-by-state surrogate-decision-maker hierarchies. State-by-state involuntary-commitment statutes (e.g., 5150 / 5250 in California, IPC in Texas, Baker Act in Florida). State-by-state mature-minor doctrine and minor-consent-for-specific-services laws (STIs, contraception, mental health, substance use). These are real exam content but vary by jurisdiction; NCLEX-RN tests the federal-floor principles, and prep treatment of the variants belongs in a state-aware overlay if SME wants one.

### Tricky distractors

- **Minor vs Emancipated minor.** Both are below the age of majority by chronological age. Wrong-answer pattern: choosing "parent must sign" for an emancipated 17-year-old. Emancipation transfers consent authority to the minor; parents are no longer the legal decision-makers.
- **Unconscious adult emergency vs Cognitively impaired.** Both involve a patient unable to consent in the moment. Wrong-answer pattern: invoking implied-consent emergency doctrine for a chronically cognitively impaired patient with a known healthcare proxy. Implied consent applies only when delay would cause death or serious harm AND no representative is reachable; cognitive impairment with a known proxy uses the proxy.
- **Mental-health hold ≠ blanket loss of consent rights.** A patient on an involuntary hold retains the right to refuse most non-emergency treatments unless court-ordered for that specific treatment. Wrong-answer pattern: "patient is on a 72-hour hold, so the nurse can give any ordered medication without consent." Wrong — the hold authorizes detention for evaluation and emergency intervention, not blanket treatment consent.
- **Family member as interpreter.** The federal floor requires information in language understandable to the patient `[s4]`; in clinical practice, qualified medical interpreters are the standard, and using family members (especially minor children) is widely deprecated. Wrong-answer pattern: choosing "use the patient's adult daughter to interpret consent" — the federal regulation requires understandability, and clinical standards require *qualified* interpretation; the daughter is neither qualified nor neutral. (Specific HHS / Title VI / Section 1557 LEP requirements are state-and-payer-specific and land in the unsourced-values table.)
- **Implied consent does not cover non-emergent procedures.** A patient unconscious from anesthesia for a different procedure cannot have a new procedure added under "implied consent" unless the new procedure meets the immediate-harm threshold. Wrong-answer pattern: extending implied consent to convenience additions during surgery.
- **Assent vs Consent.** A capable minor's assent is sought alongside (not instead of) parental permission in research `[s5]` and is the standard clinical norm for minors. Wrong-answer pattern: treating a 12-year-old's verbal agreement as legal consent — assent supports ethical practice; legal consent still comes from the parent or guardian unless the minor is emancipated.
