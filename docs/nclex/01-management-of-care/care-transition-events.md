# Care Transition Events

**Topic:** 1 — Management of Care &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Pattern:** Dimensions &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Tags:** 1.9, IP-CD, IP-TL
**Status:** draft (SME review pending)

The five care-transition events NCLEX-RN tests under sub-objective 1.9 (Continuity of Care). Rows are the events; columns are shared attributes drawn from the federal regulations that govern each transition (42 CFR 482 / 489 series), AHRQ Patient Safety Network primers on handoffs and discharge, and the AHRQ Patient Safety and Quality nursing handbook chapter on handoffs. This is the master comparison Concept; the structured handoff frame itself (SBAR letters and content) lives in `sbar-handoff-structure.md`, and discharge readiness criteria in detail live in `discharge-readiness-criteria.md`.

## Notes

- **Cell convention:** each `<br>`-separated item is one atomic Fact. No parentheticals in cells.
- **Why these five Rows.** NCSBN's 1.9 Continuity of Care covers the transitions a hospitalized client passes through during a single episode of care. Admission opens the episode; intra-facility transfer (e.g., ED to floor, floor to ICU, or step-down) and shift handoff move responsibility within the facility; inter-facility transfer moves the client to a different hospital or post-acute facility under EMTALA when the sending hospital cannot meet the client's needs; discharge closes the episode. Each transition is a federally-regulated handoff point with its own documentation floor.
- **Why the four Columns.** The Topic README columns (`required documentation`, `handoff method`, `client-education focus`, `common omission`) capture the four NCLEX-testable dimensions: what artifact must exist, who hands off to whom, what the client must understand, and what most often gets dropped. They are stable across all five Rows.
- **Cross-Concept links.** Sibling `sbar-handoff-structure.md` (1.6) covers the four SBAR letters as the structure used during handoff; this Concept covers the events themselves. Sibling `discharge-readiness-criteria.md` (1.9) covers the criteria a client must meet to be safely dischargeable; this Concept treats discharge as one Row in the comparison. Sibling `interdisciplinary-team-members.md` (1.6) covers who-hands-off-to-whom by role; this Concept covers what-must-be-handed-off by transition type.
- **Why "Structured tool such as SBAR or I-PASS" is one Fact, not two.** AHRQ PSNet `[s6]` lists I-PASS as gold standard and SBAR as the dominant nursing frame; the Fact in this Concept is the *use of a structured tool*, not the choice between them. The two named tools are the canonical instances that satisfy that Fact, and SBAR has its own Concept for letter-by-letter probing.
- **Bedside handoff specifically.** AHRQ PSNet and the Patient Safety and Quality handbook both note that bedside handoff (with the client present) reduces information loss compared to reports given away from the bedside; bedside handoff is named as a Fact in shift handoff and intra-facility transfer where it is the contemporary recommended practice [s6, s8].
- **Medication reconciliation appears in three Rows.** AHRQ PSNet `[s9]` describes medication reconciliation as required at admission, at transfer, and at discharge — three of the five transition events. The omission of reconciliation appears as a `common omission` Fact at all three of those transitions.
- **EMTALA scope.** The inter-facility transfer Row reflects an unstabilized-emergency transfer governed by 42 CFR 489.24 (EMTALA). Stable inter-facility transfers (e.g., to a SNF or rehab on discharge) are governed by 42 CFR 482.43's discharge-planning rules and are folded into the Discharge Row, not duplicated here. SME may want to surface the unstable-vs-stable distinction in Notes for `discharge-readiness-criteria.md`.
- **NCSBN exam framing.** The 2026 NCLEX-RN Test Plan references "Continuity of Care" at 1.9 as the testable construct without enumerating each transition's documentation list. `[s1]` is cited at the registry level for exam scope; per-cell citations come from the federal regulations and AHRQ primers that NCSBN test items mirror.
- **Read-back verification.** Read-back (receiver repeats critical information back to sender) is a separate safety step that runs during or after a handoff regardless of structure [s8]. It appears as one Fact in shift handoff because that's where it most often surfaces in NCLEX items; the same verification applies to all five transitions and is captured in `sbar-handoff-structure.md` Notes.

### Tricky distractors

- **Inter-facility transfer vs intra-facility transfer.** Wrong-answer pattern: treating an ED-to-ICU move as inter-facility. Inter-facility means a different hospital or post-acute facility under EMTALA `[s5]`; intra-facility means a different unit within the same hospital. Documentation requirements diverge sharply (physician certification and receiving-facility acceptance are EMTALA-only).
- **Discharge vs inter-facility transfer to a SNF or rehab.** Wrong-answer pattern: applying EMTALA documentation to a stable post-acute transfer. Stable post-acute transfers are governed by 42 CFR 482.43 discharge planning `[s4]`; EMTALA `[s5]` applies when the client is unstabilized and the sending hospital cannot meet their needs.
- **H&P timing.** Wrong-answer pattern: stating the H&P must be completed *before* admission. The federal rule allows up to 30 days before *or* up to 24 hours after admission `[s2]`, but in either case before any procedure requiring anesthesia.
- **Patient rights notice.** Wrong-answer pattern: deferring rights notice until the client is settled on the unit. The rule is "in advance of furnishing or discontinuing patient care whenever possible" `[s3]`, which means at admission registration.
- **Discharge teach-back.** Wrong-answer pattern: equating reading discharge instructions aloud with teach-back. Teach-back requires the client to *demonstrate* or *restate* understanding in their own words `[s7]`; reading instructions to the client without verification is not teach-back.
- **Code status and allergies as common omissions.** These appear as common omissions in both admission and shift handoff because they are the two most-cited missing-information categories in handoff studies `[s8]`. Wrong-answer pattern: choosing "vital signs" as the most common omission — vitals are routinely documented; high-stakes static facts like code status are routinely missed.
