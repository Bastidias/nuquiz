# Informed Consent Roles

**Topic:** 1 — Management of Care &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Pattern:** Dimensions &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Tags:** 1.12, IP-CD
**Status:** draft (SME review pending)

The five canonical actors in a normal-case informed-consent transaction. Rows are roles; columns capture each role's substantive responsibility, whether the role must be physically present at the consent encounter, and whether the role must sign the consent form. Federal floor: 42 CFR 482.13(b)(2) (patient rights — informed decisions) `[s2]` and 42 CFR 482.51(b)(2) (a properly executed informed consent form must be in the chart before surgery, except in emergencies) `[s3]`. Procedural detail (who explains, what the form lists, witness requirement) comes from CMS State Operations Manual interpretive guidelines for those CFR sections `[s4]` and AHRQ "Making Informed Consent an Informed Choice" training materials `[s5]`. Sibling Concept `consent-edge-cases.md` covers the populations whose pathway departs from the normal case (minors, emancipated minors, unconscious-adult emergencies, cognitively impaired, mental-health holds, non-English speakers); this Concept covers the routine actors only.

## Notes

- **Cell convention:** each `<br>`-separated item is one atomic Fact. No parentheticals in cells.
- **Row label decision — "Practitioner performing procedure".** The Topic 1 README spec proposed `Provider (MD/NP/PA performing procedure)` for this Row. The parenthetical fails two of the parens-safety rules in `knowledge-map.md` — it contains a comma-list of credentials (MD / NP / PA) and it smuggles a separate attribute ("performing procedure" qualifies which provider). Resolved by renaming to the CMS-canonical phrase `Practitioner performing procedure` `[s4]` (singular noun phrase, no parens, no list). The MD / NP / PA enumeration moves to this Notes bullet rather than into a `who qualifies` Column, because none of the other Rows (RN, Client, Legal representative, Interpreter) has analogous credentialing content — a single-cell Column would be a worse structural fit than a Notes bullet. **Disciplines that may serve as the practitioner under CMS guidance:** physician (MD/DO), independent nurse practitioner, independent physician assistant — the practitioner who orders the test, prescribes the treatment, or performs the procedure is the one responsible for the consent process `[s5]`.
- **What "informed" means at the federal floor.** 42 CFR 482.13(b)(2) `[s2]` grants the patient (or representative as allowed under State law) the right to make informed decisions, including being informed of health status, being involved in care planning, and being able to request or refuse treatment. The CFR itself does not enumerate the disclosure elements — those come from the CMS interpretive guidelines `[s4]`, which list anticipated benefits, material risks, and alternative therapies as the substantive disclosure content, plus the form-element minimums.
- **Minimum elements of a properly executed consent form (CMS interpretive guidelines).** Per `[s4]`: name of the hospital; name of the specific procedure; name of the responsible practitioner who is performing the procedure; statement that the procedure including anticipated benefits, material risks, and alternative therapies was explained to the patient or representative; signature of the patient or legal representative; name of the practitioner who conducted the informed consent discussion; date, time, and signature of the person witnessing the signing; indication or listing of the material risks discussed. The current guidelines do not require the witness to be a "professional"; this Concept's RN-as-witness Row reflects standard nursing practice and the AHRQ training-module workflow `[s5]`, not a federal mandate that the witness be an RN specifically.
- **Synthesis-by-direct-derivation — RN row.** The RN's substantive role (verify consent obtained and documented before procedure; witness the signature; reinforce teaching) is taken from the AHRQ training script `[s5]`: "The admitting nurse must verify that the physician, independent nurse practitioner, or independent physician assistant performing the procedure obtained informed consent and documented in the patient's electronic health record before treatment occurs." The cell phrasing mechanically restates that sentence; cited to `[s5]`.
- **Synthesis-by-direct-derivation — Client cells.** The "Receive disclosure in understandable language" cell mechanically restates 42 CFR 482.13(b)(2)'s right to be informed `[s2]` combined with the CMS interpretive-guideline expectation that disclosure be made in a manner the patient can understand `[s4]`. Cited to `[s2]` with the reading note that the "understandable language" framing is sharpened by `[s4]`.
- **Synthesis-by-direct-derivation — Interpreter row.** AHRQ `[s5]` states: "Qualified interpreters who interpreted an informed consent discussion, or sight-translated the informed consent form, or both, must also sign the form. In the case of telephone interpreters, the physician, independent nurse practitioner, or independent physician assistant conducting the discussion may write the interpreter's name or identification number on the form." The four interpreter-row cells split this single AHRQ sentence into four atomic Facts.
- **Cross-Concept links.** Sibling `consent-edge-cases.md` (1.12, 1.14) covers the populations whose pathway departs from the normal case — minors, emancipated minors, unconscious-adult emergencies, cognitively impaired, mental-health holds, non-English speakers. The Legal representative Row here references the surrogate role; the edge-cases Concept enumerates which populations actually invoke that surrogate. Sibling `advance-directive-types.md` (1.1) covers the document types (living will, healthcare proxy, POA-HC, POLST/MOLST, DNR) whose existence determines who the legal representative is. Sibling `hipaa-disclosure-rules.md` (1.8) covers separate authorization requirements for protected-health-information disclosure; informed-consent-for-procedure and HIPAA-authorization-for-disclosure are different consent transactions.
- **Out of scope.** The disclosure-elements detail (specific risks per procedure, "reasonable patient" vs "reasonable practitioner" disclosure standard, materiality threshold) is jurisdictional case law, not federal CFR content. The witness-must-be-a-professional rule was removed from CMS interpretive guidelines in 2007 and is out of scope here. Telehealth-specific consent (separate informed consent for telehealth itself, in some states) is out of scope. Anesthesia consent as a separate signed form (common practice, varies by facility) is out of scope. Research consent (45 CFR 46) is out of scope — sibling `consent-edge-cases.md` cites that body for analogous principles only.

### Tricky distractors

- **Who obtains consent.** The practitioner performing the procedure conducts the consent discussion. Wrong-answer pattern: choosing "the RN obtains informed consent." The RN witnesses the signature and verifies the form is in the record; the RN does not obtain consent for a procedure another provider will perform `[s5]`.
- **What the RN witness is attesting to.** The RN's signature as witness attests to the patient's signature being voluntary and that the patient is the person signing — NOT that the disclosure was adequate. Wrong-answer pattern: "the RN witness signs to confirm the patient understood the risks." The CMS interpretive guidelines `[s4]` describe the witness as the person witnessing the patient or legal representative signing the consent form; the disclosure-adequacy attestation is the practitioner's, signaled by the form's "statement that the procedure including anticipated benefits, material risks, and alternative therapies was explained."
- **RN identifies new questions or doubts.** If the patient expresses confusion or new questions during the witnessing step, the RN stops the signature, notifies the provider, and asks the provider to return to re-clarify. Wrong-answer pattern: "the RN explains the procedure's risks to clear up the patient's confusion" — the substantive disclosure is the provider's role, not the RN's.
- **Family-member as interpreter.** AHRQ `[s5]` and CMS `[s4]` describe qualified interpreters as the standard. Using family members (especially minor children) for consent interpretation is widely deprecated. Wrong-answer pattern: choosing "ask the patient's adult son to interpret" when a qualified interpreter is available.
- **Phone interpreter vs in-person interpreter.** In-person interpreters sign the form. Telephone interpreters do not sign — the practitioner records the interpreter's name or ID number on the form `[s5]`. Wrong-answer pattern: requiring a phone interpreter's signature.
- **"Properly executed" does not require a professional witness.** Pre-2007 CMS guidance required a professional witness. The current interpretive guidelines `[s4]` do not. Wrong-answer pattern: rejecting a consent form because the witness was a non-clinical staff member; the current guideline does not require professional status.
- **Legal representative vs healthcare proxy.** The Legal representative Row in this Concept is a generic actor — under State law, this may be a healthcare proxy (previously designated by the patient), a court-appointed guardian, a surrogate decision-maker via state hierarchy, or a parent for a minor. The edge-cases Concept handles which Row applies to which population.
