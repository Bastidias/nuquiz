# Fall Risk Factors and Mitigations

**Topic:** 2 — Safety and Infection Prevention and Control &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Pattern:** Dimensions &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Tags:** 2.1, IP-CJ
**Status:** draft (SME review pending)

The eight fall-risk factors that drive most exam-tested screening and intervention decisions in older adults. Rows are individual risk factors; columns are the four shared attributes the CDC STEADI algorithm and AHRQ Patient Safety Network frame around — the underlying mechanism, the validated screening or assessment tool, the targeted clinical mitigation, and the environmental / structural control. STEADI [s2] is the primary source for community-dwelling adults 65+; AHRQ PSNet [s7] is the primary source for inpatient-bundle interventions where STEADI is silent (post-fall huddles, hourly rounding, sitters, bed alarms).

| Risk factor | risk mechanism | screening tool | mitigation | environmental control |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Age >65 | More than 1 in 4 adults 65+ falls each year [s2] | Stay Independent 12-question screener at risk if score ≥4 [s2]<br>Annual fall-risk screen [s2] | Educate on fall prevention [s2]<br>Assess vitamin D intake [s2]<br>Refer to community exercise program [s2] | Remove clutter at home [s6]<br>Remove tripping hazards [s6] |
| History of falls | One prior fall predicts another [s6] | Three key questions: fallen in past year [s2]<br>Stay Independent fall-history item [s6] | Full multifactorial assessment [s2]<br>Refer to evidence-based fall prevention program [s2] | Post-fall huddle to identify cause [s7]<br>Nonslip floors [s7] |
| Sedating meds | Drowsiness from sleep, anxiety, pain, BP medicines [s5]<br>Loss of balance from medicine side effects [s5]<br>Slower reaction time from medicine side effects [s5] | Beers Criteria medication review [s2]<br>STEADI-Rx pharmacist medication review [s5] | Stop offending medication [s2]<br>Switch to safer agent [s2]<br>Reduce dosage [s2] | Remove deliriogenic drugs from regimen [s7] |
| Orthostatic hypotension | Drop in systolic BP ≥20 mm Hg on standing [s4]<br>Drop in diastolic BP ≥10 mm Hg on standing [s4]<br>Lightheadedness or dizziness on standing [s4] | Lying BP after 5 minutes supine [s4]<br>Standing BP at 1 minute [s4]<br>Standing BP at 3 minutes [s4] | Stop or reduce BP-lowering medication [s2]<br>Encourage adequate hydration [s2]<br>Foot-pump exercises [s2]<br>Compression stockings [s2]<br>Establish appropriate BP goal [s2] | Slow position changes at bedside [needs source] |
| Visual impairment | Reduced acuity contributes to falls [s2]<br>Poor depth perception contributes to falls [s2] | Snellen eye test [s2] | Refer to ophthalmologist or optometrist [s2]<br>Stop or reduce vision-affecting medication [s2]<br>Consider cataract surgery [s2]<br>Educate on single-vision vs multifocal lenses [s2] | Adequate room lighting [needs source] |
| Gait instability | Weak leg muscles cause falls [s6]<br>Unsteadiness when walking signals poor balance [s6]<br>Steadying on furniture signals poor balance [s6] | Timed Up and Go ≥12 seconds [s3]<br>30-Second Chair Stand [s2]<br>4-Stage Balance Test [s2] | Refer for physical therapy [s2]<br>Refer to evidence-based exercise program [s2]<br>Tai Chi [s2] | Footwear with traction and proper fit [s2]<br>Refer to OT for home safety evaluation [s2] |
| Toileting urgency | Rushing to bathroom increases fall chance [s6]<br>Nighttime toileting increases fall chance [s6] | Stay Independent toilet-rush item [s6] | Scheduled continence management [s7]<br>Frequent toileting assistance [s7] | Bedside commode placement [s7]<br>Clear path to bathroom [s7] |
| Cognitive impairment | Delirium increases fall risk [s7]<br>Depression symptoms linked to falls [s6] | Identify comorbidities including depression [s2] | Optimize treatment of comorbidities [s2]<br>Delirium prevention bundle [s7]<br>Discontinue deliriogenic medications [s7] | Sitter for high-risk patient [s7]<br>Bed alarm [s7]<br>Lowered bed height [s7] |

## Notes

- **Cell convention:** each `<br>`-separated item is one atomic Fact. No parentheticals in cells.
- **Row label `Age >65`.** The `>65` operator is the canonical CDC STEADI age cutoff for community-dwelling fall-risk screening [s2] and is the population definition CDC uses across STEADI materials. Treated as part of the row identifier rather than a parenthetical.
- **Why these eight rows.** STEADI's algorithm screens by age (65+) and a small set of three key questions, then assesses a defined set of modifiable risk factors: gait/strength/balance, fall-risk medications, home hazards, orthostatic BP, visual acuity, footwear, vitamin D, and comorbidities including depression [s2]. The eight rows here collapse STEADI's branches into the eight that NCSBN Topic 2.1 most often tests.
- **Two-source split per cell.** STEADI is community-dwelling and outpatient-oriented; AHRQ PSNet [s7] is inpatient/acute-care-oriented. The `screening tool` and `mitigation` columns are dominated by STEADI; the `environmental control` column draws more from AHRQ where the inpatient bundle (post-fall huddle, sitter, bed alarm, scheduled toileting) is the standard.
- **Stay Independent score threshold.** A patient is at risk for falling if they score ≥4 on the 12-item Stay Independent questionnaire [s6]; the questionnaire was validated by Rubenstein et al. and is reproduced on the CDC STEADI brochure.
- **Three key questions.** STEADI offers an alternative ultra-short screen — "Feels unsteady when standing or walking?", "Worries about falling?", "Has fallen in past year?" — patient is at risk if YES to any [s2]. Both the Stay Independent and the Three Questions tools are listed as acceptable initial screens in the algorithm.
- **Orthostatic vs standing BP.** STEADI's orthostatic measurement protocol is supine 5 minutes, then BP and pulse standing at 1 and 3 minutes; abnormality = systolic drop ≥20 mm Hg, diastolic drop ≥10 mm Hg, or symptomatic lightheadedness [s4].
- **Cross-Concept links.** This Concept covers the risk-factor × intervention matrix. Sibling Concepts cover adjacent material: `restraint-alternatives.md` (T2 §2.10) covers behavioral and environmental alternatives to restraint that overlap fall mitigation; `safe-patient-handling.md` (T2 §2.3) covers transfer and ambulation technique; `home-safety-by-population.md` (T2 §2.5) covers age-stratified hazards including older-adult home falls. Out of scope here: post-fall medical workup, fracture assessment, hip-protector evidence.

### Tricky distractors

- **TUG threshold.** ≥12 seconds is the STEADI cutoff for "at risk for falling" [s3]. Wrong-answer pattern: 14 sec or 10 sec. Anything under 12 sec is not flagged as abnormal by STEADI.
- **Orthostatic threshold timing.** STEADI requires standing measurements at both 1 and 3 minutes [s4]. Wrong-answer pattern: a single standing measurement at 2 minutes or "immediately on standing." The 3-minute reading captures delayed orthostatic hypotension that the 1-minute reading can miss.
- **Stay Independent score.** ≥4 is the at-risk threshold [s2]. Wrong-answer pattern: ≥3 (off by one), ≥6 (too high — would underscreen).
- **Beers Criteria scope.** Beers identifies potentially inappropriate medications in older adults; STEADI references it for fall-risk medication review [s2]. Wrong-answer pattern: claiming Beers is a fall-risk score (it is a medication appropriateness list, not a numeric risk score).
- **STEADI-Rx pharmacist role.** STEADI-Rx is a pharmacist-facing extension of STEADI; pharmacists screen, perform medication review, and share information with the prescriber [s5]. Wrong-answer pattern: assuming only the physician or RN performs the medication review.
- **Cognitive impairment vs depression.** STEADI lists depression among comorbidities to optimize, and the Stay Independent screener includes a depression-symptom item [s2,s6]. AHRQ PSNet emphasizes delirium specifically in inpatients [s7]. Wrong-answer pattern: treating "cognitive impairment" as a single homogeneous risk; STEADI and AHRQ each frame it differently (chronic comorbidity vs acute delirium).

- `? | mitigation → Refer for physical therapy` → Gait instability
