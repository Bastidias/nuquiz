# Surgical (Sterile) vs Medical Asepsis

**Topic:** 2 — Safety and Infection Prevention and Control &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Pattern:** Dimensions &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Tags:** 2.9, IP-CJ, IP-CD
**Status:** draft (SME review pending)

Medical asepsis (clean technique / aseptic technique) and surgical asepsis (sterile technique) are the two infection-control postures the nurse selects between based on whether the procedure breaches the body's normal sterile barriers. Medical asepsis aims to *reduce* the number of pathogens and prevent their transfer between persons or objects; surgical asepsis aims to *eliminate* all microorganisms within and around a sterile field. NCLEX-RN tests this distinction under sub-objective 2.9 — the nurse must pick the correct technique for the procedure, recognize when sterility has been broken, and act on the breach.

## Notes

- **Cell convention:** each `<br>`-separated item is one atomic Fact. No parentheticals in cells.
- **Aseptic vs sterile naming.** The StatPearls / Nursing Skills source uses "aseptic technique" as a synonym for medical asepsis and "sterile technique" as a synonym for surgical asepsis. NCLEX-RN test-plan language at §2.9 names "Surgical Asepsis" explicitly; "medical asepsis" is the conventional paired counterpart. Both naming conventions appear in NCLEX-style scenarios.
- **Question-form synthesis.** Several When-used and Technique-marker Facts paraphrase the source's described procedures into NCLEX-style nurse-action language (e.g., source: "Sterile technique is needed for any invasive procedure, including … Foley catheter insertion" → Fact: "Urinary catheter insertion"). These are sourced cells with mechanical wording transformation, not pedagogical inference.
- **Breach response asymmetry.** Medical asepsis breach is recoverable in-line (re-wash, re-glove, swap supply) and the procedure continues. Surgical asepsis breach in a critical step (e.g., contaminated catheter at a sterile insertion) requires discarding the contaminated item AND replacing it with a fresh sterile one — not just re-cleansing the hands. AHRQ's CAUTI-tool transcript captures this asymmetry directly.
- **Maximal sterile barrier carve-out.** CDC's CLABSI prevention guideline elevates central-line insertion from ordinary surgical-asepsis precautions (sterile gloves + drape) to "maximal sterile barrier" (mask, cap, gown, sterile gloves, full-body drape). Captured as a Fact in the Surgical asepsis × Technique markers cell. NCLEX has tested this explicitly.
- **Cross-Concept links.** Sibling `sterile-field-maintenance.md` (2.9) drills into the geometry of the sterile field (1-inch border, above-waist, line-of-sight, moisture, motion rules) — Surgical asepsis × Technique markers above lists the field-edge and below-table rules at summary level; the sibling Concept is the deeper drill. Sibling `ppe-donning-doffing-sequence.md` (2.9) covers the donning order that supports surgical asepsis. Sibling `hand-hygiene-five-moments.md` (2.9) covers when to perform hand hygiene; this Concept covers what KIND of antisepsis (standard vs surgical scrub).

### Tricky distractors

- **Urinary catheter insertion (sterile) vs urinary catheter routine care (clean).** Insertion of an indwelling urinary catheter is surgical asepsis — sterile gloves, sterile drape, sterile catheter kit. Once in place, daily catheter care (perineal hygiene, securing the tubing) is medical asepsis. Wrong-answer pattern: choosing clean gloves for catheter insertion or sterile gloves for routine perineal care.
- **Peripheral IV start (clean) vs central line insertion (maximal sterile barrier).** Peripheral IV starts use medical asepsis with a skin-antiseptic prep. Central line insertion uses surgical asepsis at the maximal-barrier level. Wrong-answer pattern: applying central-line precautions to peripheral IVs or vice versa.
- **Wound dressing change — clean vs sterile.** A simple chronic-wound dressing change in a stable home-care patient may be clean technique; a fresh post-operative or invasive wound is sterile technique. The source distinguishes "removing a contaminated dressing" (clean technique with non-sterile gloves for self-protection) from "applying" a sterile dressing (sterile gloves). Order matters.
- **Asepsis vs antisepsis vs disinfection vs sterilization.** Asepsis is the goal-state (free of pathogens). Antisepsis is the action of reducing microbes on living tissue (e.g., chlorhexidine skin prep). Disinfection reduces microbes on inanimate objects to safe levels (e.g., bleach wipe on a bedside table). Sterilization eliminates ALL microbes including spores on inanimate objects (e.g., autoclaved instruments). NCLEX uses these terms precisely.
