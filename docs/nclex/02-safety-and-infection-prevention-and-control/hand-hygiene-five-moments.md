# Hand Hygiene Indications (WHO 5 Moments)

**Topic:** 2 — Safety and Infection Prevention and Control &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Pattern:** Ordered &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Tags:** 2.9, IP-CJ, IP-TL
**Status:** draft (SME review pending)

The WHO "My 5 Moments for Hand Hygiene" framework defines the five clinical indications where the healthcare worker must perform hand hygiene to prevent pathogen transfer between the patient zone and the healthcare zone. Moments 1 and 2 protect the patient from organisms on the worker's hands; Moments 3, 4, and 5 protect the worker and the wider healthcare environment from organisms acquired from the patient or the patient surroundings. NCLEX-RN tests this under sub-objective 2.9 (Standard Precautions / Transmission-Based Precautions / Surgical Asepsis) as the canonical timing framework for hand hygiene.

**Layout convention:** rows are the five WHO moments in their published 1 → 5 order, which also reflects the temporal flow of a typical patient-zone encounter (entering → procedure → fluid exposure → leaving patient → leaving surroundings). Columns progress left → right from the contextual cue that triggers the moment (Trigger), to the recommended cleansing technique for that moment (Method), to the typical compliance failure that breaks the moment (Failure mode). Each `<br>`-separated item is one atomic Fact.

## Notes

- **Cell convention:** each `<br>`-separated item is one atomic Fact. No parentheticals in cells.
- **Patient zone vs healthcare zone.** WHO defines the patient zone as the patient and their immediate surroundings (bed, side table, equipment in continuous direct contact). Crossing the patient-zone boundary inbound triggers Moment 1; crossing it outbound triggers Moment 4 (after patient touch) or Moment 5 (no patient touch). This zone construct is the underlying logic for why Moments 1, 4, and 5 exist, captured in the Trigger column rather than as a separate Row.
- **Soap-and-water as override, not alternative.** CDC and WHO both designate alcohol-based handrub as the preferred default for routine clinical situations. Soap-and-water is required as an override when a specific condition is present (visibly soiled hands, suspected/confirmed spore-forming or non-enveloped-virus pathogen, certain contact precautions). The Method cells list the alcohol-based default last so the override conditions appear first when triggers apply.
- **Bacillus anthracis carve-out.** Alcohol-based handrub is inactive against bacterial spores. CDC's anthrax guidance specifies handwashing with soap and water (or 2% chlorhexidine gluconate) for 30–60 seconds after suspected anthrax-spore exposure. Moment 3 is the canonical hand-hygiene moment for spore exposure.
- **Glove use does not replace hand hygiene.** Gloves reduce but do not eliminate hand contamination, and hands may be contaminated during glove removal. Both WHO and CDC explicitly require hand hygiene immediately after glove removal — captured as a Fact in Moment 3's Trigger column and as a Failure mode for Moment 3.
- **Method column header.** The Topic 2 README spec proposed `Method (soap vs. alcohol)` for this column. The parenthetical was dropped on authoring because the cell values themselves enumerate the options without ambiguity, and the trigger condition that drives method selection lives in the Trigger column where it can be cell-shared across Rows. Keeping the header as `Method` preserves atomicity in the column scope.
- **Question-form synthesis.** Trigger and Failure-mode cells paraphrase the source's described situations into NCLEX-style nurse-action language (e.g., source: "before clean/aseptic procedures, e.g., oral/dental care, wound dressing" → Facts: "Immediately before a clean or aseptic task" / "Includes oral or dental care" / "Includes wound dressing change"). These are sourced cells with mechanical wording transformation, not pedagogical inference.
- **Cross-Concept links.** Sibling `ppe-donning-doffing-sequence.md` (2.9) covers the PPE sequence that surrounds the aseptic moments — hand hygiene is the first step of donning and the last step of doffing, and the link between Moment 3 and post-doffing hand hygiene is the cross-Concept tie. Sibling `chain-of-infection.md` (2.9) frames hand hygiene as the canonical break-the-chain intervention at the mode-of-transmission link.

### Tricky distractors

- **Moment 1 vs Moment 5.** Both involve crossing the patient-zone boundary. Moment 1 is on entry (protect the patient); Moment 5 is on exit without patient touch (protect the environment). Wrong-answer pattern: choosing Moment 4 instead of Moment 5 because the worker was at the bedside, even though the patient was never touched.
- **Moment 3 vs Moment 4.** Both occur after patient contact. Moment 3 is triggered by body-fluid exposure or glove removal regardless of whether the encounter is over; Moment 4 is triggered by leaving the patient. A single encounter can require both (e.g., remove dressing → Moment 3 → continue care → leave bedside → Moment 4). Wrong-answer pattern: collapsing them into one cleansing.
- **Alcohol-based handrub vs soap and water for C. difficile.** CDC's published preference remains alcohol-based handrub for routine care because clinical studies have not shown a CDI rate change tied to method, AND removing alcohol-based handrub from the workflow risks lowering overall compliance. However, soap and water is recommended in C. difficile outbreaks and during direct C. difficile patient care because alcohol does not kill spores. NCLEX answer convention follows the soap-and-water guidance for C. difficile contact precautions; do not pick alcohol-based handrub for that scenario even though CDC's preferred-default language can read that way out of context.
- **Donning sterile gloves does not replace Moment 2.** Hand hygiene immediately precedes donning sterile gloves; the glove is the sterile barrier, not a substitute for the cleansing.
