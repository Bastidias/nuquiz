# Hazardous Material Categories

**Topic:** 2 — Safety and Infection Prevention and Control &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Pattern:** Dimensions &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Tags:** 2.4, IP-CJ
**Status:** draft (SME review pending)

The six hazardous-material streams a bedside nurse most often handles, each with a distinct disposal stream, PPE expectation, spill protocol, and regulating authority. NCLEX 2.4 (Handling Hazardous and Infectious Materials) tests recognition of which container goes with which waste, what PPE level is required, and which authority sets the rule — confusion across these streams (e.g., putting chemotherapy waste in a red biohazard bag) is a high-yield distractor pattern. The federal floors here are real and citable: OSHA 29 CFR 1910.1030 governs bloodborne-pathogen waste; USP <800> and NIOSH govern hazardous-drug handling; EPA and DOT govern mercury and chemo bulk waste under RCRA; the NRC governs radioactive material under 10 CFR Parts 20 and 35.

## Notes

- **Cell convention:** each `<br>`-separated item is one atomic Fact. No parentheticals in cells.
- **Row-label decision: `Biohazardous waste` rather than `Biohazard (red bag)`.** The README spec was `Biohazard (red bag)`. Stripping the parenthetical leaves a clean Row label that does not collide with any other Row, so option (a) (canonical-name exemption) was technically defensible. Chose option (b) anyway: the red-bag detail is a Fact about the disposal Column, not a Row identity. Putting it in the container/disposal column makes the Fact engine-distractable (a student can be tested on whether biohazardous waste goes in red, yellow, or black). Leaving it in the Row name would have collapsed that Fact into the Row identifier where the engine cannot probe it.
- **Why "Chemotherapy waste" and "Hazardous drug spill" are separate Rows.** They share much of the PPE and regulatory framework (both governed by USP <800> and NIOSH), but the disposal stream and protocol diverge: routine chemo waste is a planned disposal flow (yellow container, trace contamination), while a spill is an emergency-response sequence (restrict access, eye/skin flush thresholds, warning signs). Dimensions Pattern requires Rows that compare cleanly on shared attributes; these two compare cleanly because the attributes differ in load-bearing ways.
- **Trace vs bulk chemotherapy waste.** USP <800> and EPA RCRA together establish a two-stream system. Trace contamination (gloves, gowns, empty IV bags, syringes with residual drug) goes in the yellow chemotherapy waste container for incineration at a regulated medical waste facility [s6]. Bulk waste (P-listed arsenic trioxide, U-listed bulk amounts, expired vials, partial-dose syringes containing more than RCRA-empty residue) must go to a black RCRA hazardous waste container for incineration at an EPA/RCRA-permitted facility [s6]. Bedside nurses dispose primarily of trace; bulk goes through pharmacy.
- **Yellow vs black vs red containers — the high-yield confusion.** Red biohazard bag = bloodborne / infectious waste under OSHA. Yellow chemotherapy container = trace chemo waste under USP <800>. Black container = RCRA bulk hazardous waste under EPA. Sharps containers are typically red but the OSHA standard requires "labeled or color-coded" (biohazard symbol), not specifically red. Mixing these up — putting a chemo-contaminated glove in a red bag — is a documented citation finding under USP <800>.
- **Mercury thermometers in modern hospitals.** Most US hospitals have phased out mercury thermometers, sphygmomanometers, and esophageal dilators per Joint Commission and CMS environmental safety initiatives. The Concept retains mercury because (a) NCLEX still tests the cleanup protocol, (b) home-care settings still encounter mercury thermometers, and (c) the do-NOT-vacuum / do-NOT-broom rule is the canonical exam answer. The EPA cleanup steps in the spill cell are what's tested.
- **Radiation source PPE is contextual, not generic.** The film badge / TLD dosimeter is the universal radiation-worker PPE [s8]; lead aprons are protocol-dependent (more often used for fluoroscopy than for sealed-source brachytherapy). The Radiation Safety Officer (RSO) directs PPE selection per institutional license — the cell captures the universal items and points to the RSO for variance.
- **Tag note.** Carrying `IP-CJ` (Clinical Judgment) because waste-stream routing is a recognize-cues / take-action decision the bedside nurse owns. The Topic 2 README lists `IP-CJ` as one of the primary Integrated Process Tags for the Topic. `IP-TL` (Teaching/Learning) was a defensible second Tag because much of this content is taught to staff and patients (e.g., home mercury cleanup), but the operative test focus is the nurse's own clinical-judgment routing decision; chose `IP-CJ` only.
- **Cross-Concept links.** Sibling `chemotherapy-handling.md` (Topic 2, Aspects pattern) elaborates the chemotherapy stream into the route-specific procedures, exposure response, and pregnancy precautions; this Concept covers chemo at the category level only. Sibling `isolation-precaution-types.md` covers the OSHA Bloodborne Pathogens framework as it applies to standard-vs-transmission-based PPE; this Concept covers the waste-disposal slice of the same standard. Sibling `reportable-event-categories.md` covers the post-event reporting flow when a spill or needlestick occurs. Out of scope here: specific drug names on the NIOSH List of Hazardous Drugs (Topic 6 territory), specific isotope half-lives or radiation dose tables (out of scope for nursing-level NCLEX), state-specific medical-waste manifest paperwork.

### Tricky distractors

- **Chemo waste in a red bag.** Wrong-answer pattern: "Place the used chemotherapy IV tubing in the red biohazard bag." Wrong — chemotherapy waste goes in the yellow chemotherapy waste container, not red. Red is reserved for bloodborne / infectious waste under OSHA 1910.1030. Mixing the streams is a USP <800> citation.
- **Vacuuming or sweeping mercury.** Wrong-answer pattern: "Vacuum the area to collect the mercury beads quickly." Wrong on two counts — vacuuming aerosolizes mercury and contaminates the vacuum; sweeping breaks beads into smaller droplets that spread further. The correct tools are stiff paper or cardboard, eyedropper for droplets, and sticky tape for small beads [s3].
- **Recapping a contaminated needle.** Wrong-answer pattern: "Recap the needle before placing it in the sharps container." Wrong — OSHA 1910.1030 prohibits recapping by hand because most needlestick injuries occur during recapping; place the needle directly into the sharps container without recapping [s2].
- **Bare hands to retrieve a dislodged radioactive source.** Wrong-answer pattern: "Pick up the dislodged seed and place it back in the patient's applicator." Wrong — never touch a sealed source with bare hands. Use long forceps and place the source in a lead-lined shielded container, then notify the Radiation Safety Officer immediately [s7].
- **N95 vs surgical mask for chemo aerosol.** Wrong-answer pattern: "A standard surgical mask is sufficient when administering inhaled or aerosol chemotherapy." Wrong — NIOSH-certified N95 or N100 respirator is required when aerosolization is possible [s4]. Surgical masks are not respirators and do not provide a tested seal against airborne particulates.
- **Pouring mercury down a drain.** Wrong-answer pattern: "Wash the mercury beads down the sink with running water." Wrong — mercury can lodge in plumbing and contaminate sewage treatment or septic systems; it must be collected and disposed of as hazardous waste per local authority [s3].
- **Treating a hazardous-drug spill like a routine chemo-waste disposal.** Wrong-answer pattern: "Bag the contaminated linens in a yellow chemotherapy container and continue care." Wrong — a spill triggers the spill-kit protocol: restrict access, post warning signs, full PPE before approach (chemo gloves, gown, N95, eye protection), contain with absorbent pads, then dispose in the appropriate container [s4, s5]. The spill response precedes routine disposal.
