# Equipment Safety Check Sequence

**Topic:** 2 — Safety and Infection Prevention and Control &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Pattern:** Ordered &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Tags:** 2.7, IP-CJ, IP-CD
**Status:** draft (SME review pending)

The canonical pre-use sequence a nurse runs on any patient-care device — infusion pump, ventilator, telemetry monitor, suction unit, PCA pump, feeding pump, sequential compression device — before connecting it to a client. NCSBN's 2026 RN test plan §2.7 (Safe Use of Equipment) names the entry-level activities directly: "Inspect equipment for safety hazards (e.g., frayed electrical cords, loose/missing parts)", "Facilitate appropriate and safe use of equipment", "Remove malfunctioning equipment from client care area and report the problem to appropriate personnel", and client-teaching about safe equipment use `[s1]`. The six-row sequence below operationalizes those statements as a deterministic checklist. ECRI inspection procedures (the de-facto US baseline for clinical-engineering pre-use testing) cover the same physical-integrity, alarm-performance, output, electrical-safety, and mechanical-integrity dimensions across 150+ device types, performed "before the devices are put into use for the first time and then on a routine schedule" `[s6]`. Two cross-cutting hazards drive most testable failures: alarms run on manufacturer defaults instead of patient-individualized parameters `[s3]` and equipment is connected to a client whose identity was not verified against two identifiers `[s4, s5]`.

**Layout convention:** rows are the six pre-use steps in canonical order, numbered. Columns progress left → right from what the nurse does at this step (Action), through the recurring NCLEX-tested mistake at this step (Common omission), to what goes wrong clinically when the omission occurs (Failure consequence). Each `<br>`-separated item is one atomic Fact.

## Notes

- **Cell convention:** each `<br>`-separated item is one atomic Fact. No parentheticals in cells.
- **Slash-row-name canonical-pair exemption.** Row 2 is labeled `Verify settings/alarms`. Settings (programmed rate / dose / mode) and alarms (thresholds / volume / priority) are taught and checked as a single canonical step in the standard nursing pre-use sequence — separating them would create two adjacent rows with overlapping Action cells and break the column-strip parens-safety rule (both rows would expose the same shared "Action" Values around programming verification). Slash preserves the canonical pairing per knowledge-map atomicity exception for canonical compound names.
- **Device-agnostic framing.** The six rows are written generically because §2.7 itself is device-agnostic — NCSBN tests the *sequence* and the *omissions*, not infusion-pump-specific or ventilator-specific controls. Specific device types (PCA, smart pumps, ventilators, telemetry, oxygen delivery) get their own Concepts under Topic 6 (`pca-pump-safety.md` proposed) and Topic 7. This Concept's Rows scope to the universal pre-use steps; sibling Concepts will reuse the row labels with device-specific Action cells.
- **Why six rows and not five or seven.** The README spec lists six. The pedagogical sequence collapses cleanly to "physical → settings → power → consumables → client → documentation" — five would force settings and alarms to merge into power (loses the alarm-individualization beat that drives `[s3]`'s entire literature), seven would split documentation into pre-and-post (post-use is a separate Concept covering shift-end documentation, not pre-use).
- **Cross-Concept link — `alarm-fatigue-mitigation.md` (sibling under §2.7).** This Concept names *that alarms must be individualized at step 2*; the sibling Aspects Concept covers individualization mechanics, alarm-priority hierarchy, response expectations, and escalation.
- **Cross-Concept link — `incident-report-rules.md` (T1) and `reportable-event-categories.md` (T2 §2.6).** Step 1's "Sequester the device if signs of breakage are present" and the parallel "Remove malfunctioning equipment from client care area and report the problem to appropriate personnel" activity statement `[s1]` connect to the incident-reporting workflow. This Concept names *that the device is removed*; the sibling Concepts name *the report mechanics*.
- **Cross-Concept link — `mar-and-tar-charting.md` (T1, when authored).** Step 6's documentation Facts are the equipment-side sliver of the broader medication-administration-record / treatment-administration-record charting Concept. This Concept names *what to capture at pre-use*; the sibling Concept names *how MAR/TAR is structured and timed*.
- **Synthesis-by-direct-derivation disclosure.** "Common omission" cells are derived from the corresponding Action cells by mechanical inversion (Action: "Confirm programmed rate against the order" → Omission: "Single-nurse programming check on high-alert infusions" via `[s7]`'s double-verification framing). "Failure consequence" cells are derived from sourced safety-event findings cited per Fact. These are sourced cells with a transformation, not unsourced Facts.
- **Two-identifier rule scoping.** Step 5 applies the Joint Commission two-identifier rule. Acceptable identifier pairs are `name + DOB`, `name + MRN`, and equivalents; room number and bed location are explicitly excluded `[s4]`. The full identifier-pair Concept (`patient-identification-rules.md`, T1, when authored) enumerates pairs and edge cases (newborns, unconscious clients, language barriers); this Concept references the rule at the equipment-connection moment only.
- **Out of scope.** Routine post-use cleaning and high-level disinfection (covered in T2 §2.9 disinfection Concepts), preventive-maintenance scheduling (clinical-engineering domain, not nursing pre-use), end-of-life device disposition, and biomedical engineering bench testing (the "before the devices are put into use *for the first time*" arm of `[s6]` — this Concept covers the routine-shift pre-use arm). Device-specific alarm-class definitions (high-priority red flashing vs medium-priority yellow vs low-priority cyan per IEC 60601-1-8) are part of `alarm-fatigue-mitigation.md`, not here.

### Tricky distractors

- **Inspect for damage vs Verify settings/alarms — sequencing.** Wrong-answer pattern: programming the device before confirming physical integrity. A cracked housing or frayed cord identified after settings are programmed costs the time spent programming and risks initiating the device on a damaged unit. Physical integrity is a gating check; programming is downstream.
- **Verify power source vs Verify supplies — sequencing.** Wrong-answer pattern: hanging the bag and threading tubing before plugging in. If the device is on battery and the battery is depleted, the entire setup must be redone. Power-source verification gates supply setup.
- **Two-identifier rule — what counts.** Wrong-answer pattern: choosing room number or bed letter as the second identifier. Room and bed are location identifiers, not patient identifiers — they are explicitly excluded. Acceptable second identifiers are date of birth or medical-record number (paired with the client's name).
- **Default alarm parameters vs individualized parameters.** Wrong-answer pattern: accepting the pump's manufacturer-default alarm thresholds because "they came that way." Manufacturer defaults are population-level baselines; the individual client's clinical baseline is what determines actionable alarm thresholds. This is the alarm-fatigue root cause.
- **Documentation timing.** Wrong-answer pattern: documenting the pre-use check at the end of shift from memory. Pre-use baseline must be captured at the time of the check — not reconstructed later. The legal MAR / TAR depends on real-time entry.
- **Sequester vs continue using.** Wrong-answer pattern: continuing to use a device with a small chip or unexplained alarm because "it still seems to work." `[s2]` is explicit: small chips, cracks, or unexplained alarms trigger sequestration, not continued use.
