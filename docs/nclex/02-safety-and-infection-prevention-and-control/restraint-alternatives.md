# Restraint Alternatives

**Topic:** 2 — Safety and Infection Prevention and Control &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Pattern:** Dimensions &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Tags:** 2.10, 1.5, IP-CJ
**Status:** draft (SME review pending)

Less-restrictive interventions that nurses must consider or attempt before initiating physical or chemical restraint, per 42 CFR 482.13(e)(2): "Restraint or seclusion may only be used when less restrictive interventions have been determined to be ineffective to protect the patient, a staff member, or others from harm" [s1]. Rows are the seven canonical alternatives named in NCSBN sub-objective 2.10 framing and AHRQ patient-safety guidance; Columns capture each alternative's typical indication, the bedside signal that tells the nurse the alternative is working, and the criterion that justifies escalating to a restraint order. Sibling Concept `01-management-of-care/restraint-use-rules.md` covers what happens after escalation — order rules, monitoring frequency, max duration. This Concept covers what comes before.

| Alternative | indication | effectiveness signal | escalation criterion |
|---|---|---|---|
| Bed/chair alarm | High fall risk [s2]<br>Impulsive attempts to stand or transfer unassisted [s3]<br>Cognitive impairment with intact mobility [s2] | Alarm sounds and staff arrive before patient leaves bed or chair [s2]<br>No fall events during shift [s3] | Patient defeats or removes alarm repeatedly [s2]<br>Falls occur despite alarm activation [s2]<br>Behavior escalates to violent or self-destructive [s1] |
| Sitter | Acute agitation with high fall or self-harm risk [s4]<br>Suicidal ideation requiring 1:1 observation [s4]<br>Active delirium with line- or tube-pulling [s5] | Patient remains in bed or chair safely with sitter present [s4]<br>No interference with lines, tubes, or dressings [s5]<br>Verbal de-escalation succeeds [s5] | Patient harms self or others despite sitter presence [s1]<br>Sitter cannot physically prevent imminent harm [s1]<br>Behavior meets violent or self-destructive threshold [s1] |
| Diversional activity | Boredom-driven agitation [s5]<br>Restlessness in cognitively impaired patient [s5]<br>Pre-procedure anxiety [s5] | Patient engages with activity for sustained period [s5]<br>Reduction in calling out or wandering [s5]<br>Lower observed agitation score [s5] | Patient cannot be redirected [s5]<br>Agitation escalates despite redirection [s1]<br>Imminent harm risk [s1] |

| Re-orientation | Delirium [s5]<br>Postoperative confusion [s5]<br>ICU-acquired disorientation [s5] | Patient correctly states name, place, or time after prompting [s5]<br>Reduced calling out for unfamiliar persons [s5]<br>Cooperates with care after orientation cues [s5] | Persistent disorientation despite repeated cues [s5]<br>Behavior becomes violent or self-destructive [s1]<br>Active interference with life-sustaining treatment [s1] |

## Notes

- **Cell convention:** each `<br>`-separated item is one atomic Fact. No parentheticals in cells.
- **Why this Concept exists.** 42 CFR 482.13(e)(2) makes "less restrictive interventions… determined to be ineffective" a precondition for any restraint use, and 42 CFR 482.13(e)(16)(iii) requires that "alternatives or other less restrictive interventions attempted" be documented in the medical record [s1]. NCLEX tests this prerequisite directly under sub-objective 2.10 (Use of Restraints / Safety Devices). This Concept enumerates the alternatives so a student can match each to its typical indication and recognize when escalation is justified.
- **Bed/chair alarm row label.** The slash is a canonical-pair exemption. Bed alarms and chair alarms are functionally one intervention category in nursing curriculum — the device is named for its placement. Splitting them into two Rows would duplicate every cell verbatim and add no engine signal.
- **Effectiveness signal column.** Each value is a bedside observation a nurse can document on a flowsheet within one shift. Cells deliberately avoid outcome metrics that require systems-level data (e.g., facility fall rate). This keeps the column tractable for cross-Row comparison and for engine question generation.
- **Escalation criterion column.** Many cells reference 42 CFR 482.13(e)'s "violent or self-destructive behavior that jeopardizes physical safety" threshold [s1]. That phrasing is the federal criterion that unlocks behavioral-emergency restraint and seclusion. Where cells cite [s1] for escalation, the escalation criterion is being matched to the federal indication for restraint in the sibling Concept.
- **Sibling: `01-management-of-care/restraint-use-rules.md`.** Sibling covers what happens after the alternatives in this Concept have been exhausted: order requirements, monitoring frequency, max order duration by age tier, and documentation. This Concept covers what comes before and the criteria for moving to the sibling.
- **Out of scope.** Pharmacologic alternatives that are not chemical restraint (e.g., scheduled antipsychotics for diagnosed psychosis, PRN analgesia for diagnosed pain) are not Rows here. Per 42 CFR 482.13(e), a drug used at standard treatment dose for a diagnosed condition is not a restraint and so is not an "alternative to restraint" — it is treatment of the underlying condition [s1]. Chemical restraint itself is a Row in the sibling Concept, not here.
- **Out of scope.** Environmental modifications that are not interventional (e.g., low bed, non-slip footwear, decluttered room) are part of fall-prevention practice [s2][s3] but are not "alternatives to restraint" in the federal sense — they are baseline environment, not interventions deployed in response to a behavior trigger. Listed here for SME consideration only; flag if SME wants them added as a Row.

### Tricky distractors

- **Sitter vs family presence.** Both are 1:1 bedside human presence, but the sitter is a paid staff member assigned for safety observation, while family presence is an unpaid family member offering reassurance. The discriminator on NCLEX: if the stem says "the nurse arranges for continuous observation," that's a sitter; if the stem says "the daughter agrees to stay with the patient," that's family presence. Both are alternatives to restraint, but they are different Rows because the indication and the escalation criterion differ.
- **Re-orientation vs diversional activity.** Re-orientation addresses cognitive disorganization (the patient does not know where they are); diversional activity addresses behavioral restlessness (the patient knows where they are but cannot tolerate sitting still). A delirious patient gets re-orientation first; a bored cognitively-intact patient gets a diversional activity first.
- **Bed/chair alarm is not a restraint.** Common misconception. An alarm that notifies staff when a patient stands is a monitoring device, not a restraint — it does not restrict the patient's freedom of movement. Federal rules apply only to interventions that restrict movement [s1]. A bed-exit alarm is therefore an alternative to restraint, not a form of it.
- **Toileting schedule = purposeful rounding.** Some sources call this "scheduled toileting" or "continence management" or "purposeful rounding" or "hourly rounding with the 4 Ps" (pain, position, potty, possessions). NCLEX accepts these as the same intervention. The Row label here uses NCSBN-style "toileting schedule" framing; clinical synonyms are equivalent answers.
- **Reposition for comfort vs reposition for pressure injury prevention.** The Row here is reposition-for-behavior-management — a patient is restless because they're in pain or uncomfortable, so changing position settles them. Reposition-for-pressure-injury-prevention (q2h turning) is a different indication and lives in pressure-injury Concepts (sibling `pressure-injury-staging.md`). NCLEX may test the discrimination.

## Engine demo opportunities

- `Bed/chair alarm | indication → ?` → High fall risk, Impulsive attempts to stand or transfer unassisted, Cognitive impairment with intact mobility (multi-Fact cell)
- `Sitter | escalation criterion → ?` → Patient harms self or others despite sitter presence, Sitter cannot physically prevent imminent harm, Behavior meets violent or self-destructive threshold (multi-Fact cell)
- `? | indication → Delirium` → Re-orientation (single-Row select; Row isolation)
- `? | escalation criterion → Behavior escalates to violent or self-destructive` → Bed/chair alarm, Toileting schedule (cross-Row select-all; tests the federal-threshold pattern)
- `Family presence | effectiveness signal → ?` → Patient calms in family member's presence, Reorients to person and place when family speaks, Cooperates with care while family present (multi-Fact cell)
- `Re-orientation | ? → Delirium` → indication (column-hide form)
- Composite Row profile: Re-orientation Row with the `indication` cell swapped to "Boredom-driven agitation" (a Diversional activity Value) — tests the re-orientation vs diversional discrimination called out in Tricky distractors.
- `? | indication → Cognitive impairment with intact mobility` vs `? | indication → Cognitive impairment with intact continence` — distinguishes Bed/chair alarm Row from Toileting schedule Row on the precise mechanism the alternative is targeting.
