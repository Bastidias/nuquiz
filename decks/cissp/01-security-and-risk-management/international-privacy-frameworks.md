# International Privacy Frameworks

**Domain:** 1 — Security and Risk Management &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Pattern:** Dimensions &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Tags:** 1.4, 1.5
**Status:** draft (SME review pending)

Four international frameworks and agreements that govern cross-border personal-data flows. Distinct from the sibling `privacy-laws` Concept (which covers specific national statutes such as GDPR, CCPA, HIPAA, PIPEDA, LGPD) — this Concept covers the *transnational instruments* that those statutes interact with: a binding Council of Europe treaty, a non-binding OECD recommendation, an APEC regional accountability scheme, and the bilateral EU-US adequacy mechanism that replaced the invalidated Privacy Shield. CISSP testing emphasizes which framework binds whom, what enforcement teeth (if any) it carries, and the Schrems lineage of the EU-US arrangements.

## Notes

- **Cell convention:** each `<br>`-separated item is one atomic Fact. No parentheticals in cells.
- **Acronym expansions.** `OECD` = Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development. `APEC` = Asia-Pacific Economic Cooperation. `CBPR` = Cross-Border Privacy Rules. `CPEA` = Cross-border Privacy Enforcement Arrangement. `EEA` = European Economic Area. `FTC` = US Federal Trade Commission. `CJEU` = Court of Justice of the European Union. `DPF` = Data Privacy Framework.
- **Convention 108 is the only legally binding international privacy treaty.** Originally adopted by the Council of Europe in 1981 and entered into force 1985. Open to ratification by non-CoE states (Uruguay, Mauritius, Senegal, Argentina, Mexico, Morocco, and Tunisia have all acceded), making it the closest thing to a global privacy treaty. Convention 108+ (2018 modernization) aligns the text with GDPR — adding breach notification, accountability, and rights against algorithmic decisions — and requires 38 of the 55 Parties to ratify before it enters force; as of early 2026 it is in the high-30s and not yet fully effective.
- **OECD Guidelines are foundational but non-binding.** The 1980 OECD Guidelines articulated the eight Fair Information Practice Principles (FIPPs) that subsequent regimes — Convention 108, EU Directive 95/46, GDPR, APEC Framework, US sectoral law — all echo. The 2013 revision *kept the eight original principles intact* and added concepts like privacy management programmes, breach notification, national privacy strategies, and global interoperability. Tested point: the eight principles count and names come from OECD 1980, not GDPR.
- **APEC Privacy Framework operates through CBPR certification.** The Framework itself is a set of nine principles (one more than OECD — adds "Preventing harm"). Operationalization is via the CBPR system: companies self-assess against the principles, get certified by an Accountability Agent, and the certification is mutually recognized across participating economies. CBPR participating economies: US, Mexico, Japan, Canada, Korea, Singapore, Chinese Taipei, Philippines, Australia. The Global CBPR Forum (April 2022) spun the system out of APEC to allow non-APEC economies (notably the UK) to join.
- **DPF is the third attempt at EU-US adequacy.** Safe Harbor (2000) was invalidated by *Schrems I* (CJEU C-362/14, October 2015). Privacy Shield (2016) replaced it, then was invalidated by *Schrems II* (CJEU C-311/18, July 2020). DPF (July 2023) is the third iteration; it added a Data Protection Review Court and Executive Order 14086 limits on US signals-intelligence collection to address Schrems II's concerns. The EU General Court upheld DPF in *Latombe* (T-553/23, September 3, 2025); Latombe appealed to the CJEU on October 31, 2025 — as of 2026, the CJEU appeal is pending and NOYB has signaled a possible "Schrems III" challenge.
- **Frameworks vs laws.** This Concept's frameworks are *transnational coordination instruments*. The frameworks themselves usually do not directly grant rights to individual data subjects — they bind states (Convention 108) or organizations through self-certification (DPF, CBPR) or set policy norms (OECD). The directly-enforceable rights live in domestic implementing law: GDPR, UK DPA 2018, Japan APPI, US state laws, etc. Sibling Concept `privacy-laws` covers those.
- **Out of scope for this Concept:** EU Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs) and Binding Corporate Rules (BCRs) — these are *transfer mechanisms* under GDPR Article 46, not standalone frameworks. UK extension to the DPF (UK-US "data bridge"), Swiss-US DPF, Japan-EU mutual adequacy, and bilateral adequacy decisions in general. ASEAN Framework on Personal Data Protection. African Union Convention on Cyber Security and Personal Data Protection (Malabo Convention). Chinese PIPL cross-border rules and the standard contract certification system.

### Tricky distractors

- **Convention 108 vs GDPR.** Convention 108 (1981) is *older* and *more broadly ratified* — 55 Council of Europe parties plus several non-European accessions. GDPR (2018) applies only to EU/EEA but is much more detailed and enforcement-heavy. Convention 108+ (2018) is the GDPR-aligned modernization but has not yet fully entered force. CISSP exam point: don't equate "international privacy law" with GDPR — Convention 108 predates it by decades and binds more countries.
- **Privacy Shield vs DPF.** Privacy Shield was the *second* EU-US framework (2016), invalidated by *Schrems II* in July 2020 over US surveillance and lack of EU-resident redress. DPF is the *third* framework (July 2023), with the Data Protection Review Court and EO 14086 added specifically to fix Schrems II's defects. Naming a CISSP question's framework "Privacy Shield" in 2026 is the trap — it has been invalid for over five years.
- **OECD Guidelines (non-binding) vs Convention 108 (legally binding).** OECD Guidelines are a *recommendation* — voluntary, no enforcement, no treaty obligation. Convention 108 is a *treaty* — ratifying states must implement it in domestic law and may be held to it. The 1980 OECD Guidelines and the 1981 Convention 108 were drafted in parallel; despite the overlap in principles, only Convention 108 carries legal weight on its signatories.
- **APEC Framework principle count (9) vs OECD principle count (8).** APEC adds "Preventing harm" as an explicit ninth principle. CISSP exam may ask "how many principles" or list a principle and ask which framework — "Preventing harm" is the APEC giveaway.
- **Safe Harbor / Privacy Shield / DPF chronology.** Safe Harbor 2000–2015 (invalidated *Schrems I*); Privacy Shield 2016–2020 (invalidated *Schrems II*); DPF 2023–present (currently valid, under appeal). Three frameworks, two invalidations, one Schrems family.
