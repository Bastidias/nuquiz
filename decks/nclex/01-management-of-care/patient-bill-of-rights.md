# Patient Bill of Rights Elements

**Topic:** 1 — Management of Care &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Pattern:** Dimensions &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Tags:** 1.5, 1.2, IP-CG, IP-CD
**Status:** draft (SME review pending)

The seven core "Patient Bill of Rights" principles tested on NCLEX. There is no single canonical "Patient Bill of Rights" document — the Affordable Care Act consumer bill of rights, the AHA Patient Care Partnership (which replaced the 1973 AHA Patient's Bill of Rights), the CMS Conditions of Participation patient rights at 42 CFR 482.13, the Medicare beneficiary rights, and various state versions all overlap on the same principles. NCLEX tests the principles, not a specific document. Rows are the seven principles named in the Topic 1 §1.5 README; columns are nurse-relevant attributes (what the right guarantees, the nurse's obligation, a common violation).

## Notes

- **Cell convention:** each `<br>`-separated item is one atomic Fact. No parentheticals in cells.
- **No single canonical "Patient Bill of Rights" document.** The phrase appears in at least four overlapping instantiations: (a) the Affordable Care Act consumer protections (CMS, 2010), (b) the AHA Patient Care Partnership (which replaced the 1973 AHA Patient's Bill of Rights), (c) the CMS Conditions of Participation patient rights at 42 CFR 482.13 (the federal floor for Medicare-participating hospitals), and (d) state-specific patient bills of rights. The seven principles in the Rows are the durable cross-document common denominator that NCLEX tests. Cells default to the CMS COP language when a principle is named there; AHA Partnership and Medicare beneficiary rights fill principles the COP does not name explicitly (choice of providers, access to emergency services).
- **Why the synthesized framing.** Topic 1 §1.5 specifies these seven Rows; this Concept files them under one comparable group rather than authoring seven separate Concepts. The federal regulation (42 CFR 482.13) provides direct citations for five of the seven Rows; the remaining two (choice of providers, access to emergency services) draw on Medicare beneficiary rights at medicare.gov and EMTALA framing.
- **EMTALA reference (out of scope for this Concept, sibling for later).** The Emergency Medical Treatment and Labor Act (42 U.S.C. §1395dd) is the federal statute behind the "access to emergency services" Row's stabilization rule; cited here as [s4] but not enumerated as its own Row. EMTALA-specific obligations (medical screening exam, stabilization, transfer rules) belong in a Topic 1 or Topic 2 Concept of their own once SME confirms scope.
- **AHA Patient Care Partnership.** AHA replaced the 1973 Patient's Bill of Rights with the Patient Care Partnership in 2003. Several "respect / non-discrimination" and "information disclosure" cells lean on its plain-language framing of care expectations; cited as [s2].
- **State-law variance.** State patient bills of rights add detail (e.g., right to an itemized bill, right to refuse research participation, right to specific timeframes for record access). NCLEX defaults to federal language; state-specific items not drawn from CMS or AHA are out of scope here. Tag `state-variance` deliberately NOT applied — the federal floor (42 CFR 482.13) is substantive and directly testable; this Concept does not depend on state-defined rules the way `mandatory-reporting-categories` or `consent-edge-cases` do.
- **HIPAA cross-link.** Confidentiality Row obligations overlap with HIPAA disclosure rules (sibling Concept `hipaa-disclosure-rules.md`, T1 §1.8). This Concept covers confidentiality as a patient right; the sibling covers the disclosure-rule mechanics (treatment / payment / operations / public-health exceptions). Tag overlap: `1.5` and `1.8` cross-reference.

### Tricky distractors

- **Information disclosure vs informed consent.** Information disclosure is the right to be told about diagnosis and treatment options in plain terms; informed consent is the legal/procedural artifact for invasive interventions. Sibling Concept `informed-consent-roles.md` (T1 §1.12) covers consent procedure. Wrong-answer pattern: choosing "obtain informed consent" when the stem describes a patient asking about their diagnosis — that's information disclosure, not consent.
- **Choice of providers under Medicare Advantage.** Original Medicare lets the beneficiary see any Medicare-certified provider; Medicare Advantage plans can require in-network providers and referrals [s3]. NCLEX wrong-answer pattern: claiming an Advantage-plan patient has unrestricted choice.
- **EMTALA "stabilization" vs definitive treatment.** EMTALA requires stabilization before transfer or discharge, not definitive cure [s4]. Wrong-answer pattern: refusing transfer of a stabilized patient on the grounds that they have not received definitive treatment.
- **Refusal of treatment with capacity.** A patient with decisional capacity may refuse any treatment, including life-sustaining treatment [s1]. Wrong-answer pattern: treating against an informed refusal because "the family insists" or "the provider believes it's best."
- **Grievance vs complaint vs incident report.** A grievance is a formal patient complaint requiring written hospital response [s1]; a complaint can be informal and resolved at the bedside; an incident report is an internal safety document and is NOT shared with the patient. Wrong-answer pattern: telling the patient their incident report will be sent to them.
- **Family notification of admission requires patient consent.** 42 CFR 482.13(b)(4) gives the patient the right to have a family member or representative AND their own physician notified — but the regulation says "of his or her choice," meaning the patient designates [s1]. Wrong-answer pattern: notifying family without checking the patient's preference.
