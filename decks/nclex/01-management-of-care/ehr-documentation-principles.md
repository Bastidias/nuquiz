# EHR Documentation Principles

**Topic:** 1 — Management of Care &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Pattern:** Aspects &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Tags:** 1.13, 1.8, IP-CD
**Status:** draft (SME review pending)

EHR documentation is the legal, contemporaneous record of nursing assessment, intervention, and outcome captured in the electronic health record. It is a single artifact with multiple facets — what every entry must contain, when entries must be made, how mistaken entries are corrected, how delayed entries are added, and what security controls protect the record from unauthorized access or alteration. NCLEX tests this primarily under sub-objective 1.13 (Information Technology) and 1.8 (Confidentiality / Information Security); CMS Conditions of Participation (42 CFR 482.24) and the HIPAA Security Rule (45 CFR 164.308–164.312) are the canonical US regulatory frameworks.

## Notes

- **Cell convention:** each `<br>`-separated item is one atomic Fact. No parentheticals in cells.
- **Aspects single-Row scope qualifiers.** Per `knowledge-map.md` Aspects parens-safety carve-out, scope qualifiers stay inline because splitting them into a separate Column would create a Column carrying only one cell. Examples kept inline: "after a period of inactivity" (automatic logoff), "within 30 days of discharge" (discharge summary). No actual parentheses appear in cells.
- **CMS and HIPAA are the regulatory floor.** 42 CFR 482.24 (CMS Conditions of Participation) sets the federal floor for what entries must contain — legible, complete, dated, timed, authenticated. 45 CFR 164.308 (administrative safeguards) and 164.312 (technical safeguards) set the federal floor for security controls. State Nurse Practice Acts and facility policy add layers on top; the NCLEX tests the federal/predominant-convention floor.
- **Procedural specifics for paper-era corrections.** Several error-correction Facts (single line strikethrough, initialing, dating the correction, "no correction fluid") originate in paper-record era guidance and are perpetuated by AHIMA practice briefs and facility policy rather than a single canonical federal rule. They remain testable on NCLEX because the underlying principle — preserve the original entry, identify who corrected it and when — is reflected in 45 CFR 164.526 (amendment must link to the affected record). Marked `[needs source]` for SME pass; the underlying principle is sourced.
- **Late entry vs amendment.** "Late entry" is the term for documenting care that was delivered earlier in the same encounter but not charted contemporaneously. "Amendment" (45 CFR 164.526) is the term for changing or appending information after the record is finalized. NCLEX uses "late entry" for the nursing-shift use case; both require date/time of entry, identification of the author, and preservation of the original record.
- **Copy-and-paste hazard.** AHRQ PSNet identifies copy-and-paste as a documentation safety risk: "Copy-and-paste and other content import features can propagate incorrect, outdated, or improperly attributed information." NCLEX tests this as a documentation-accuracy and authorship-attribution principle — the nurse who signs the entry is accountable for its content, even if pulled forward from a prior note.
- **Cross-Concept links.** Sibling `hipaa-disclosure-rules.md` (1.8) covers the disclosure-side rules of confidentiality (TPO, public health, court order). Sibling `incident-report-rules.md` (1.14) covers documentation of safety events and the explicit rule that the incident report is NOT in the medical record. Sibling `sbar-handoff-structure.md` (1.6) covers the handoff communication structure. Out of scope here: ONC EHR Certification Criteria, the AHRQ Common Formats data-element schema, and clinical-content-specific charting (e.g., pain reassessment timing, IV site assessment timing) which live in respective Topic Concepts.
- **NCLEX 1.13 vs 1.8.** 1.13 (Information Technology) covers the use-of-the-system principles: documentation rules, system access, error correction, late entry. 1.8 (Confidentiality / Information Security) covers the protect-the-information principles: who may access PHI, password discipline, audit, sanctions. The security rule cell here straddles both; the dual Tag reflects that.

### Tricky distractors

- **Late entry vs falsification.** A late entry labeled "late entry" with both timestamps (when care occurred, when documentation is being entered) is acceptable and required when documentation is delayed. Backdating an entry to make it appear contemporaneous is falsification and is a Nurse Practice Act violation. Wrong-answer pattern: nurse enters a 7 AM assessment at 11 AM and dates the entry 7 AM without the late-entry marker.
- **Error correction vs deletion.** The original (incorrect) entry must remain readable after correction so the audit trail shows what was changed and when. Wrong-answer pattern: "Delete the incorrect entry and re-enter the correct information." Wrong — original must be preserved (CMS, HIPAA amendment rule).
- **Shared passwords.** Shared login credentials defeat the unique-user-identification requirement and the audit-controls requirement. The audit log must attribute every action to one identifiable user. Wrong-answer pattern: "The charge nurse logs in for the new nurse who hasn't received credentials yet." Wrong — every user must have unique credentials.
- **Automatic logoff vs manual logoff.** Both are required practices. Manual logoff when stepping away is the nurse's responsibility; automatic logoff after inactivity is a system control under 45 CFR 164.312. Wrong-answer pattern: nurse leaves a workstation unlocked because "the system will log me off eventually." Wrong — the nurse owns the manual logoff, the automatic logoff is a backstop.
- **Copy-paste of a prior note.** The nurse who signs the new entry is accountable for its content. Pulling forward a prior assessment without re-verifying is an authorship-attribution and accuracy hazard. Wrong-answer pattern: nurse copies the prior shift's assessment forward and signs it without re-assessing.
- **EHR audit log access.** Audit logs are part of the security controls (45 CFR 164.312(b)). Routine clinical staff cannot delete or alter the audit log; doing so is a federal HIPAA violation. NCLEX may test this as a question about who is accountable when an audit log shows unauthorized access.
