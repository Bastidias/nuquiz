# Mandatory Reporting Categories

**Topic:** 1 — Management of Care &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Pattern:** Dimensions &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Tags:** 1.14, IP-CG
**Status:** draft (SME review pending)

The six categories most often tested under NCLEX 1.14 (Legal Rights and Responsibilities) where a nurse has an affirmative duty to report. Federal law sets a floor for some categories (CAPTA for child abuse, the Elder Justice Act / 42 CFR 483.12 for long-term-care elder abuse, FVPSA for IPV-services funding, the CDC nationally notifiable conditions list for surveillance); the operative reporting rules — who is mandated, to whom, and the timeframe — are predominantly state law. The cells below capture the federal floor where one exists, plus the most consistent state-law convention; the unsourced-values table flags every cell where the value is the conventional state pattern rather than a federal mandate.

| Category | who must report | to whom | timeframe |
|---|---|---|---|
| Child abuse | Healthcare professional [s1, s2]<br>Mental health professional [s2]<br>School personnel [s2]<br>Law enforcement [s2]<br>Social worker [s2] | Child Protective Services [s1, s3]<br>Law enforcement [s2] | Immediately on suspicion [s1, s3] |
| Elder abuse, long-term care setting | Covered individual [s4]<br>Owner of facility [s4]<br>Operator of facility [s4]<br>Employee of facility [s4]<br>Manager of facility [s4]<br>Agent of facility [s4]<br>Contractor of facility [s4] | Facility administrator [s4]<br>State Survey Agency [s4]<br>Adult Protective Services [s4]<br>Law enforcement [s4] | Within 2 hours if serious bodily injury [s4]<br>Within 24 hours if no serious bodily injury [s4] |
| Elder abuse, community setting | Healthcare professional [s5]<br>Mandated reporter under state law [s5] | Adult Protective Services [s5]<br>Law enforcement [s5] | Per state statute [s5] |
| Intimate partner violence | Healthcare professional in mandatory-reporting states [s6]<br>Not a federal mandate [s6, s7] | Law enforcement in mandatory-reporting states [s6] | Per state statute [s6] |
| Communicable disease | Clinician [s8, s9]<br>Laboratory [s8, s9] | State health department [s8, s9]<br>Local health department [s8, s9] | Immediately for urgent conditions [s10]<br>Within 24 hours for urgent-tier conditions [s10]<br>Within 7 days for routine conditions [s10] |
| Gunshot wound | Healthcare provider [s11]<br>Hospital personnel [s11] | Local law enforcement [s11] | Per state statute [s11] |
| Impaired colleague | Nurse with knowledge of suspected impairment [s12, s13] | Nurse manager [s12]<br>State Board of Nursing [s13, s14] | Promptly to protect patient safety [s12] |

## Notes

- **Cell convention:** each `<br>`-separated item is one atomic Fact. No parentheticals in cells. Where a Row-name carries a scope qualifier (`Elder abuse, long-term care setting` vs `Elder abuse, community setting`), the qualifier disambiguates two distinct regulatory regimes — federal CMS rules at 42 CFR 483.12 govern long-term care; community elder abuse is governed by state APS statutes only. Splitting into two Rows preserves atomicity per Row and exposes that they share a Column value (both route to APS) while diverging on timeframe and who-must-report specificity.
- **Why this Concept is dominantly state-variance.** The exam tests a federal floor that is thin: CAPTA defines child abuse and conditions federal funding on state mandated-reporter laws but does not itself name reporters or timeframes [s1]. The Elder Justice Act funds elder-abuse infrastructure but the operative reporting rule for long-term care is the CMS Conditions of Participation at 42 CFR 483.12 [s4]. FVPSA funds IPV services but contains no federal mandated-reporting requirement [s7]. The CDC notifiable conditions list is a federal surveillance protocol that flows from state public-health departments to CDC; clinician-to-state reporting is mandated by state law, not federal law [s8, s9]. Gunshot-wound reporting is statute-by-statute (48 of 50 states mandate it) [s11]. Impaired-colleague reporting routes to the state Board of Nursing under each state's Nurse Practice Act [s14]. The federal floor matters for the exam because NCLEX is a national exam — items test the consistent state convention, not jurisdiction-specific minutiae.
- **Why "Healthcare professional" and not "Nurse" in the who-must-report cells.** Mandated-reporter statutes typically enumerate broad professional categories. Nurses are mandated reporters in all 50 states for child abuse [s2]; nurses are mandated for community elder abuse in most but not all states [s5]; nurses are mandated for IPV reporting only in the small minority of states that mandate IPV reporting at all [s6]. Using "Healthcare professional" keeps the cell faithful to the source language; the nurse-specific framing is in the tricky-distractors section.
- **42 CFR 483.12 covered-individual list is granular.** The covered-individual enumeration (owner, operator, employee, manager, agent, contractor) is the regulation's literal language [s4] — it is an unusually broad list because Congress wrote it that way under Section 1150B of the Social Security Act to ensure no facility staff role could disclaim the duty. Each role is a separate atomic Fact for the engine.
- **Communicable disease is two systems, not one.** State-mandated clinician-to-public-health reporting is the operative duty for the bedside nurse; CDC surveillance via NNDSS is a state-to-federal data flow and is voluntary at the federal level [s8]. The cell captures the bedside-nurse duty (report to state/local health). The CDC tier classification (immediate / 24-hour / weekly) is the framework states generally adopt [s10], so the timeframe Facts trace to the CDC tier protocol even though the legal mandate is state.
- **Impaired-colleague reporting routes through two paths.** Path 1: report to nurse manager (workplace path), per NCSBN guidance [s12]. Path 2: report to state Board of Nursing (regulatory path), where each state's Nurse Practice Act defines the mandate [s13, s14]. Both Facts in the to-whom cell. Alternative-to-discipline (ATD) programs are a downstream BON disposition, not a reporting destination [s14]; covered in Notes only, not as a Fact.
- **Cross-Concept links.** Sibling `tort-categories-in-nursing.md` covers the legal-tort framing (failure-to-report can constitute negligence per se in mandated-reporter jurisdictions). Sibling `hipaa-disclosure-rules.md` covers the HIPAA carve-outs for public-health reporting and law-enforcement disclosures, which are the privacy mechanism that makes mandated reporting lawful without client authorization. Sibling `consent-edge-cases.md` covers minor and emergency consent, which interacts with child-abuse reporting. Out of scope here: state-by-state reporter-list comparison, the precise content required in a CPS/APS report, and post-report investigation procedures.
- **Tag note.** Carrying `IP-CG` (Caring) per the Topic 1 README assignment — the duty-to-report framing is fundamentally about protecting vulnerable populations the nurse is caring for. `IP-CD` (Communication and Documentation) is also defensible because reporting is a communication act; chose `IP-CG` per the README's prior commitment.

### Tricky distractors

- **HIPAA does not block mandated reporting.** Wrong-answer pattern: "I cannot report this to CPS without the parent's consent because of HIPAA." Wrong — HIPAA's public-health and law-enforcement exceptions explicitly permit disclosure for mandated reporting. The Concept here captures the affirmative duty; the HIPAA permission lives in `hipaa-disclosure-rules.md`.
- **Suspicion is the trigger, not confirmation.** Mandated reporters report on reasonable suspicion of abuse; they do not investigate and they do not confirm before reporting [s1, s3]. Wrong-answer pattern: "Wait until the bruising pattern is confirmed by the provider before reporting." Wrong — reasonable suspicion alone triggers the duty.
- **IPV is the exception, not the rule.** A small minority of states mandate clinician reporting of IPV between competent adults [s6]; most states do not, and the major emergency-medicine and physician-ethics bodies oppose mandatory IPV reporting on autonomy and safety grounds [s7]. Wrong-answer pattern: "The nurse must report all suspected IPV to law enforcement." Wrong in most jurisdictions; the nurse must screen, document, offer resources, and in most states report only with the client's consent or when state law specifically requires it.
- **Long-term care 2-hour rule is for serious bodily injury or any abuse allegation.** 42 CFR 483.12(c)(1) sets two timeframes: 2 hours when events involve abuse OR result in serious bodily injury, 24 hours when neither [s4]. Wrong-answer pattern: "Within 24 hours" for an alleged abuse event without serious injury — wrong; the abuse allegation alone triggers the 2-hour clock.
- **Gunshot-wound report is to law enforcement, not to a health department.** Communicable-disease reporting and gunshot-wound reporting are commonly confused as "report to public health." Wrong — gunshot wounds route to local law enforcement [s11].
- **Impaired-colleague — "report to NCSBN."** NCSBN itself has no authority over individual nurses [s13]; the destination is the state Board of Nursing where the nurse is licensed. Wrong-answer pattern: "Report the impaired colleague to NCSBN."
- **Reasonable suspicion, not certainty, also for elder abuse.** Same reasonable-suspicion threshold as child abuse in most state APS statutes [s5]. Wrong-answer pattern: "Wait for the social-work consult before reporting elder abuse" — wrong; the duty is on the nurse with the suspicion.
