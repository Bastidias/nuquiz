# Advance Directive Types

**Topic:** 1 — Management of Care &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Pattern:** Dimensions &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Tags:** 1.1, IP-CD
**Status:** draft (SME review pending)

The directive instruments NCLEX-RN tests under sub-objective 1.1 (Advance Directives). Federal floor for all of these is the Patient Self-Determination Act of 1990 (codified at 42 USC 1395cc(f) for Medicare and 42 USC 1396a(w) for Medicaid), implemented at 42 CFR Part 489 Subpart I. The CFR defines an advance directive as "a written instruction, such as a living will or durable power of attorney for health care, recognized under State law (whether statutory or as recognized by the courts of the State), relating to the provision of health care when the individual is incapacitated" [s1]. Substance — what each directive specifies, when it activates, who may execute it, how it is revoked — is set by state law; the federal regulation requires Medicare/Medicaid providers to inform adult patients of those state-law rights at admission and to document whether a directive exists [s2]. Rows are the five directive instruments listed in the Topic 1 README; columns are the shared attributes used to compare them on the exam.

## Notes

- **Cell convention:** each `<br>`-separated item is one atomic Fact. No parentheticals in cells.
- **Slash names retained as canonical nomenclature.** `POLST/MOLST` is one Row. POLST (Physician Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment) and MOLST (Medical Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment) are state-nomenclature variants of the same paradigm — same function, same activation, same revocability, same statutory basis. NCBI StatPearls lists POLST, MOLST, MOST, POST, COLST, and TPOPP as state variants of the same form [s3]. The slash is inseparable canonical nomenclature, not a smuggled compound.
- **DNR and DNI split into separate Rows.** DNR refuses CPR; DNI refuses endotracheal intubation and mechanical ventilation. AHRQ PSNet explicitly distinguishes them and notes that patients frequently conflate the two — DNI is not synonymous with permanent respiratory support, and intubation can be temporary and reversible (e.g., in asthma exacerbation) [s7]. The Topic 1 README labeled this Row `DNR/DNI`; splitting into two Rows preserves the distinct-directive boundary the engine needs to probe the confusion. Total Row count is therefore 6, not 5.
- **Durable POA for healthcare vs healthcare proxy.** These are often used synonymously in clinical and lay literature. The DPOA-HC is the *document* that designates the agent; "healthcare proxy" can refer either to the *appointed person* or to a state-specific document name (e.g., New York's "Health Care Proxy" statute). NCBI StatPearls: "A healthcare surrogate, also known as a healthcare power of attorney or a healthcare proxy, is an individual who is authorized to make medical decisions" [s5]. Kept as separate Rows per the Topic 1 README spec; the Concept therefore tests both the document/agent distinction and the state-nomenclature distinction. SME may want to collapse to one Row labeled `Durable POA for healthcare / Healthcare proxy` if the synonymy outweighs the document-vs-agent distinction.
- **Statutory basis framing.** Every Row carries a state-law basis because advance-directive *substance* is set by state statute or state common law. The federal Patient Self-Determination Act sets the *floor* (Medicare/Medicaid providers must inform adult patients of state-law rights at admission and document directive status) but does not itself prescribe what a directive can say. CFR text [s1]: "recognized under State law (whether statutory or as recognized by the courts of the State)." That phrase is reproduced in Notes rather than per-cell to avoid a parenthetical violation.
- **Revocability while competent.** Each Row's revocability cell carries the qualifier "while competent" because once a patient loses decision-making capacity, only the agent or surrogate can act. This is consistent with the CFR's framing of advance directives as instruments that govern care "when the individual is incapacitated" [s1].
- **Living will activation typically requires two physicians.** NCBI StatPearls: "Usually requires the assessment and declaration of 2 physicians stating that the patient is terminally ill, suffering from an incurable illness, or in a permanently comatose state" [s3]. State law varies on the threshold (some states require one physician; some require two; some specify "attending physician plus one consulting physician"). The Concept captures the typical case; SME may want to add a state-variation note.
- **POLST is a medical order, not an instructional directive.** This is the central exam distinction. A living will tells clinicians what the patient wants in the abstract; clinicians must interpret and translate into orders. A POLST is itself the order — actionable across care settings, including by EMS in the prehospital environment [s3]. Wrong-answer pattern: treating a POLST as advisory.
- **EMS-honored directives.** "In most states, [DNR] written orders are the only advance directives that Emergency Medical Service (EMS) providers are legally obligated to consider" [s3]. Living wills and DPOA-HC documents are not EMS-actionable in the field; only out-of-hospital DNR or POLST/MOLST is. AHRQ PSNet adds: in-hospital DNR orders "are not valid after the patient is discharged or transferred to another hospital, a nursing home, or the community" [s8] — the out-of-hospital DNR is a separate document in most states.
- **Federal vs state floor.** The Patient Self-Determination Act, 42 USC 1395cc(f) (Medicare) and 1396a(w) (Medicaid), implemented at 42 CFR Part 489 Subpart I, requires Medicare/Medicaid providers to: maintain written policies on advance directives; provide written information to each adult patient at admission about their state-law rights to accept or refuse treatment and formulate a directive; document in the medical record whether the patient has a directive; not condition care on directive status; and provide community education [s2]. The Act does not itself create directive types or define their content — that is state law [s1].

Sibling `consent-edge-cases.md` (1.12) covers the unconscious-adult-emergency carve-out and the cognitively-impaired pathway, which interact with directive activation. ### Tricky distractors

- **DNR vs DNI.** Wrong-answer pattern: treating DNR as covering intubation. DNR refuses CPR; intubation outside an arrest is still on the table for a DNR-only patient [s7]. Wrong-answer pattern: assuming DNI implies permanent ventilator dependence — intubation can be brief and reversible (e.g., asthma exacerbation) [s7].
- **Living will vs POLST.** Wrong-answer pattern: treating a living will as immediately actionable in the field. A living will requires clinician interpretation and (typically) a two-physician determination that the activation condition has been met [s3]; a POLST is itself the order and is portable across settings [s3].
- **DPOA-HC vs general POA.** "Durable power of attorney" without "for healthcare" can refer to a financial-decisions instrument; the healthcare-decisions DPOA is a distinct document. Wrong-answer pattern: assuming a general POA grants healthcare-decision authority. NCBI: "the term 'durable power of attorney' is more often used to refer to an instrument appointing an agent for financial decisions" [s5].
- **Patient-Self-Determination-Act scope.** PSDA covers Medicare- and Medicaid-funded providers and HMOs (hospitals, SNFs, home health agencies, hospices, religious nonmedical health care institutions) [s2]. Wrong-answer pattern: assuming PSDA mandates that every adult must have a directive — it does not. It mandates that providers must inform patients of their state-law rights and ask whether a directive exists.
- **No discrimination based on directive status.** Providers cannot condition care or admission on whether a patient has executed a directive [s2]. Wrong-answer pattern: a hospital refusing admission because the patient declined to formulate one.
- **Healthcare proxy vs durable POA for healthcare.** State terminology varies; in many states they refer to the same instrument or to overlapping instruments [s5]. Wrong-answer pattern: insisting they are functionally distinct in every jurisdiction.
