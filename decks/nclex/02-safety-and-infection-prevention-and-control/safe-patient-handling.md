# Safe Patient Handling Techniques

**Topic:** 2 — Safety and Infection Prevention and Control &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Pattern:** Dimensions &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Tags:** 2.3, IP-TL, IP-CJ
**Status:** draft (SME review pending)

The five most-tested patient-handling techniques compared on the dimensions NCLEX-RN §2.3 (Ergonomic Principles) probes: equipment used, body mechanics, number of staff required, and contraindications. Rows are techniques (bed-to-chair transfer, repositioning in bed, boost up in bed, logroll, ambulation assist); columns are the shared attributes. Cell values capture the action level the bedside nurse must produce when assigning, performing, or teaching the maneuver. The exam-relevant pivots are (a) when manual handling is permissible vs when a mechanical lift is required, and (b) when single-staff vs multi-staff handling applies.

The Concept carries `IP-TL` because patient-handling competence is a teaching-and-learning artifact (nurse teaches family / UAP) per the Topic 2 README, and `IP-CJ` because the choice of technique is a clinical-judgment decision driven by patient weight-bearing, cognition, and stability.

## Notes

- **Cell convention:** each `<br>`-separated item is one atomic Fact. No parentheticals in cells.
- **Weight-bearing as the master pivot.** Across all five techniques, the single attribute that flips the technique from "manual handling acceptable" to "mechanical lift required" is whether the patient can bear weight bilaterally and predictably. Two-thirds of the contraindication cells reduce to this one principle [s1, s2]. Engine-relevant: the contraindication column shares values across rows where this pivot applies.
- **The 35-pound threshold.** The PMC peer-reviewed review of safe patient handling biomechanics cites the Waters / NIOSH derivation that 35 lb is the maximum recommended weight a caregiver should manually lift in the most-ideal patient-handling scenario; assistive equipment is required above that [s2]. NIOSH itself has not adopted 35 lb as a policy-based limit, and the Revised NIOSH Lifting Equation (RNLE) is not validated for lifting people [s2]. NCLEX treats the 35-lb threshold as the predominant US convention even though it is not a federal regulatory floor.
- **Mechanical lift trumps "good body mechanics."** The PMC review is explicit: "there is no way to safely manually handle patients" once the load exceeds the caregiver capacity, and proper body-mechanics training alone does not prevent injuries [s2]. The body-mechanics column captures the ergonomics for the maneuvers that ARE within manual-handling scope; it does not authorize manual handling of dependent patients.
- **Logroll is reserved for unstable-spine and post-spine-surgery patients.** It is not a general repositioning technique. Source [s4] (Logrolling: establishing consistent practice, PubMed 12024634) sets the staffing baseline at 4 minimum / 5 ideal for the supine logroll team. Air-assisted lateral transfer can substitute and requires only 2 trained personnel [s4].
- **Gait belt is the canonical ambulation tool but it has a contraindication gap.** The Nursing Fundamentals chapter [s1] does not enumerate gait-belt contraindications. Standard nursing pedagogy lists abdominal surgery, recent rib fracture, gastrostomy tube, and significant abdominal aortic aneurysm as relative contraindications, but [s1] is silent on these. Marked `[needs source]` on the gait-belt-contraindicated row.
- **Out of scope.** This Concept covers technique selection and execution. Sibling `fall-risk-factors-and-mitigations.md` (T2 §2.1) covers when to invoke any handling assistance at all (fall-risk screening). Sibling `delegable-vs-non-delegable-tasks.md` (T1 §1.3) covers which role MAY perform a transfer once the technique is selected. Sibling Topic 5 Concepts (when authored) will cover positioning for skin integrity and pressure-injury prevention.
- **NCLEX-RN exam fit.** NCSBN's 2026 NCLEX-RN Test Plan §2.3 Ergonomic Principles tests "assess client to ensure proper body mechanics," "educate client and staff regarding safe use of equipment," and "use ergonomic principles when providing care (e.g., assistive devices, proper lifting)" [s5]. The four-column dimensional cut here matches all three activity statements: equipment, body mechanics, staff number, contraindication.
- **Source allowlist note.** OSHA's *Guidelines for Nursing Homes: Ergonomics for the Prevention of Musculoskeletal Disorders* (osha.gov) is the canonical US federal guidance for SPH program design and is widely cited by AHRQ and PMC reviews — but osha.gov is not in the current NCLEX research-skill source allowlist. The 35-lb threshold and lift-equipment recommendations cited here come via the PMC review [s2], which itself cites OSHA and Waters. If osha.gov is added to the allowlist, several `[needs source]` cells (notably gait-belt contraindications) could be tightened.

### Tricky distractors

- **Single-person bed-to-chair transfer for a "cooperative" patient who cannot bear weight.** Wrong-answer pattern: "The patient is alert, oriented, and cooperative — one nurse can transfer." Wrong — cooperation alone does not satisfy the criteria. Bilateral weight-bearing is required for one-person transfer [s1]. Add this veto to the cooperation criterion mentally; the engine column splits them.
- **"Lift with your legs" as a complete answer.** Wrong-answer pattern: a question stem implying that good body mechanics make manual lifting safe for any patient. Wrong — the body-mechanics column applies only when manual handling is within scope. For dependent patients, mechanical assistance is required regardless of caregiver technique [s2].
- **Logroll vs lateral transfer.** Wrong-answer pattern: choosing logroll for any spine patient. Logroll is for spine **stabilization during turning** (e.g., post-laminectomy, suspected spinal injury); air-assisted lateral transfer is preferred when moving the patient between surfaces [s4]. The two have different staff requirements (4-5 vs 2 with device).
- **Gait belt over abdominal surgical site.** Wrong-answer pattern: "Apply the gait belt firmly around the patient's waist to assist with ambulation post-op day 1 abdominal surgery." Standard nursing pedagogy excludes this — the belt should not compress a fresh surgical incision — but the [s1] source does not state it explicitly. Flagged in the unsourced-values table.
- **Boosting a dependent patient with two nurses.** Wrong-answer pattern: assuming two staff is always sufficient. If the patient cannot assist and the boost requires lifting more than 35 lb of patient mass per caregiver, mechanical lift is indicated [s2]. The "2 minimum" cell is the floor for partially-assisting patients.

- `? | equipment used → Gait belt` → Bed-to-chair transfer, Ambulation assist (cross-Row select-all)

- `Logroll | equipment used → ?` → Slider board / Cervical collar if cervical-spine precautions / Pillows for body alignment
- Composite Row profile: `Bed-to-chair transfer` with the `# of staff` cell swapped to the logroll value (`4 minimum`) — tests recognition that staffing baselines are technique-specific.
