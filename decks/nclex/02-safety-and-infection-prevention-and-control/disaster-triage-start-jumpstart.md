# Disaster Triage (START / JumpSTART)

**Topic:** 2 — Safety and Infection Prevention and Control &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Pattern:** Ordered &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Tags:** 2.2, 1.10, IP-CJ

**Status:** draft (SME review pending)

The two paired mass-casualty triage algorithms taught in US nursing curricula. **START** (Simple Triage and Rapid Treatment) was developed by Hoag Hospital and the Newport Beach Fire Department in 1983 for adults and children eight years and older; the goal is to sort each victim into one of four color tags in roughly 30–60 seconds using respirations, perfusion, and mental status (the "RPM 30-2-can-do" mnemonic) `[s1, s2, s3]`. **JumpSTART** is the pediatric companion, developed by Lou Romig in 1995 and revised in 2001, for children under eight years; it adapts START for pediatric physiology — children are far more likely to be in respiratory failure than circulatory collapse, normal respiratory rates vary widely by age, and pre-verbal children cannot follow commands `[s1, s4, s5]`. The two algorithms share the same four-color tag system (Red immediate, Yellow delayed, Green minor, Black expectant) and the same priority-of-transport rule: Red first, Yellow second, Green last or self-care, Black not transported `[s2, s3]`. NCSBN tests these under §2.2 (Emergency Response Plan — "Use clinical decision-making/critical thinking for emergency response plan", "Determine which client(s) to recommend for discharge in a disaster situation") and §1.10 (Establishing Priorities); the candidate must distinguish disaster triage from ED triage, where the goal inverts (greatest-need-first, not greatest-survivability-per-resource) `[s6]`. This Concept holds two Ordered tables — one per algorithm — to preserve each algorithm's canonical decision sequence and surface pediatric-specific Facts NCLEX tests directly.

**Layout convention:** two Ordered tables, one per algorithm. Rows are the four triage colors in canonical priority order from highest treatment priority (Red) to no-treatment (Black), with Green walking-wounded surfaced first as the algorithmic ambulation step but ordered in the table by its transport priority. Columns progress left → right from algorithmic input (Decision criteria) through resource-allocation outcome (Treatment priority) to clinical illustration (Example presentation). Each `<br>`-separated item is one atomic Fact.

## Notes

- **Cell convention:** each `<br>`-separated item is one atomic Fact. No parentheticals in cells.
- **Two tables, one Concept — structural choice.** START and JumpSTART share Rows (the four colors) and the priority-of-transport Column but differ materially in Decision criteria and pediatric-specific Example presentation. Rendering as one combined Ordered table with a `system` Column would either (a) collapse adult-and-pediatric criteria into the same cell — destroying atomicity — or (b) duplicate every Row and triple the cell count. Two tables under H3 headers preserves each algorithm's decision sequence and lets the engine ask "JumpSTART Red × Decision criteria → ?" without leakage from the START table. This mirrors the structural choice in sibling `fire-response-race-pass.md`.
- **Atomicity decision — color row labels.** README spec lists Rows as `Black (deceased/expectant)`, `Red (immediate)`, `Yellow (delayed)`, `Green (minor)`. Rendered here as `Red immediate`, `Yellow delayed`, `Green minor`, `Black expectant` (color + canonical category name, no parens). Rationale: stripping the parenthetical from each Row exposes that the parens were carrying a separate Fact (the canonical category name) bundled into the color label. Per knowledge-map atomicity rule, parentheticals masking second Facts must be split. The two-word phrase reads as one canonical name. (`Black deceased` would also be valid; `Black expectant` chosen because in mass-casualty triage the category includes both deceased victims and expectant victims with injuries incompatible with life — "expectant" subsumes "deceased" in conventional MCI usage `[s2, s3]`.)
- **Order of table rows — priority-of-transport, not algorithm flow.** START and JumpSTART begin with an ambulation step (Green walking-wounded come out first), and the triage decision tree then sorts the non-ambulatory victims into Red / Yellow / Black by RPM/PMS criteria. This Concept tables the colors in **priority-of-transport** order (Red, Yellow, Green, Black) because that ordering reflects the ordinal scale NCLEX tests directly. Algorithm-flow ordering is described in the prose above and in the algorithm-flow Notes bullet below; tabling it here would conflict with the engine's #-column step-recall semantics (where #1 means "highest priority").
- **Algorithm flow (START).** Step 1 — direct all walking-wounded to a designated area; they are tagged Green `[s1, s2]`. Step 2 — for each remaining victim, check breathing. If apneic, position the airway once. If breathing returns, tag Red and move on. If apnea persists, tag Black `[s1, s3]`. Step 3 — assess respiratory rate. RR > 30/min = Red `[s1, s3]`. Step 4 — assess perfusion: absent radial pulse OR capillary refill > 2 seconds = Red `[s1, s3]`. Step 5 — assess mental status: cannot follow simple commands = Red; can follow commands = Yellow `[s1, s3]`. The "RPM 30-2-can-do" mnemonic encodes the cutoffs (30 breaths/min, 2 second cap refill, follows commands) `[s1]`.
- **Algorithm flow (JumpSTART).** Step 1 — ambulation; walking children tag Green; infants and other children carried out are passed to step 2 rather than auto-Green `[s4]`. Step 2 — check breathing. If apneic, position airway. If breathing returns, tag Red. If still apneic, **check pulse**: no pulse → Black; palpable pulse → give 5 rescue breaths. If breathing returns after rescue breaths, Red. If still apneic, Black `[s1, s4, s5]`. The 5-rescue-breath step is the single most-tested JumpSTART distinction. Step 3 — RR < 15 or > 45/min = Red; RR 15-45 = continue `[s1, s4]`. Step 4 — perfusion: palpable peripheral pulse (not capillary refill) `[s1, s4]`. Step 5 — AVPU: A, V, or P-appropriate = Yellow; P-inappropriate posturing or U = Red `[s1, s4]`.
- **Synthesis-by-direct-derivation disclosure.** Several Decision criteria cells in the Yellow rows are mechanical inversions of the Red criteria from the same source (source: "RR > 30 = Red" → cell: "RR ≤ 30 = Yellow"). These are sourced cells with a transformation, not unsourced Facts. Same for "follows commands = Yellow" derived from the source statement "cannot follow commands = Red." Reflexive only — no inferred facts beyond direct algorithmic complement.
- **Disaster triage vs ED triage — opposite goals.** This is the highest-yield NCLEX confusion pair. ED triage (sibling `01-management-of-care/triage-esi-levels.md`) sorts patients **most-acute-first** because resources are abundant relative to demand — the sickest patient gets the most resources first. Disaster/MCI triage sorts patients **greatest-survival-benefit-per-resource-first** because resources are scarce relative to demand — a victim whose injuries are incompatible with life (Black) gets no resources even though they are the "sickest," because resources spent there mean a Red victim dies waiting `[s2, s3]`. NCLEX items frame this as a candidate's first reaction ("treat the worst first") being wrong in the MCI context. Tag `1.10` (Establishing Priorities) is carried for this reason — the sub-objective covers prioritization frameworks broadly, including the MCI inversion.
- **Color tag pedagogy — Red/Yellow/Green/Black is canonical, but other systems exist.** The 4-tier color system is shared by START, JumpSTART, the SALT (Sort-Assess-Lifesaving-Treatment) system, and most US MCI tags. Some systems add a 5th color (Gray, sometimes Blue) for "expectant — not yet deceased but unsalvageable" distinct from Black "deceased" `[s2, s3]`. NCSBN tests the 4-color START framing; the 5-color SALT distinction is out of scope here.
- **Cross-Concept link — `01-management-of-care/triage-esi-levels.md` (sibling).** ESI is the 5-tier ED triage scheme used for routine ED arrivals; START/JumpSTART is the 4-tier MCI scheme used during a declared mass-casualty event. Different sub-objectives draw the line: 1.10 carries both because both are prioritization frameworks; 2.2 carries this Concept because MCI triage sits inside the Emergency Response Plan domain. The two are not interchangeable — applying ESI Level 1 ("immediate, multiple resources") to a mass-casualty victim with unsurvivable injuries would over-allocate resources and let salvageable victims die.
- **Cross-Concept link — `hospital-emergency-codes.md` (sibling).** Code Triage is the overhead-paged hospital code that activates the disaster plan; START/JumpSTART is what nurses do *under* a Code Triage activation. The two pair the way Code Red pairs with RACE.
- **Cross-Concept link — `fire-response-race-pass.md` (sibling).** Both Concepts live under §2.2 Emergency Response Plan and use the two-Ordered-tables-per-file structure. Fire response is a single-event facility-internal response; disaster triage is mass-casualty (often multi-victim, often pre-hospital or surge-into-hospital).
- **Out of scope.** SALT triage (the federal-recommended successor algorithm under the National MCI Guidelines `[s2]`); Sacco Triage Method; Manchester Triage System; CTAS; the specific contents of secondary-triage retriage; the legal-ethical framework for declaring a victim Black under hospital policy; and trauma-center destination decisions are not in this Concept. Pediatric vital-sign reference tables (precise normal RR by age) are out of scope — JumpSTART's 15–45 cutoff intentionally collapses age-specific norms into a single triage rule.

### Tricky distractors

- **Red vs Black on the apneic victim.** Wrong-answer pattern: tagging an apneic victim Black without a single airway-reposition attempt. START requires *one* airway repositioning before declaring Black; if breathing returns, the victim is Red. JumpSTART adds the pulse-check-and-five-rescue-breaths layer for pediatric victims with palpable pulse. NCLEX items test whether the candidate skips the airway/pulse step.
- **JumpSTART RR thresholds vs adult START.** Wrong-answer pattern: applying adult ">30/min = Red" to a child. JumpSTART uses **<15 or >45** because pediatric normal RR is higher and bradypnea is an ominous pediatric sign that adult START doesn't separately flag.
- **JumpSTART perfusion vs START perfusion.** Wrong-answer pattern: assessing capillary refill on a child for triage tag. JumpSTART uses palpable peripheral pulse, not cap refill — pediatric cap refill is unreliable in cold/dark field conditions and JumpSTART substitutes the pulse check.
- **Mental status — commands (START) vs AVPU (JumpSTART).** Wrong-answer pattern: trying to get a 4-year-old to "squeeze my hand twice." Pre-verbal children cannot follow commands as a baseline; JumpSTART substitutes AVPU for this reason. P-inappropriate or U is Red; A, V, or P-appropriate is Yellow.
- **Ambulation auto-tags Green — except for infants in JumpSTART.** Wrong-answer pattern: tagging an infant Green because a caregiver carries them out. Infants who cannot yet walk are not auto-Green; they enter the JumpSTART respiratory/perfusion/AVPU loop like any non-ambulatory victim.
- **Disaster triage inverts ED priority.** Wrong-answer pattern: spending resources on the most catastrophically injured victim "because they need help most." MCI triage allocates to the salvageable; the most catastrophically injured (Black) get comfort care or no resources. This reverses the candidate's ED-triage instinct.
- **Green ≠ uninjured.** Wrong-answer pattern: dismissing Green tags as "fine." Green victims have minor injuries and are ambulatory; they still need eventual evaluation and may deteriorate during prolonged scene operations. They are last priority for transport, not zero priority.
