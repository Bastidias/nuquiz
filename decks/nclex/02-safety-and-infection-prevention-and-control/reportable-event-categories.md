# Reportable Event Categories

**Topic:** 2 — Safety and Infection Prevention and Control &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Pattern:** Dimensions &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Tags:** 2.6, 1.14, IP-CD

**Status:** draft (SME review pending)

The six recurring event types NCLEX 2.6 ("Reporting of Incident / Event / Irregular Occurrence / Variance") tests for routing knowledge: which form gets used, who internally receives it, whether an external mandatory report is also required, and on what timeframe. This Concept sits between two siblings — `01-management-of-care/incident-report-rules.md` covers the mechanics of the internal incident report itself (what it includes, what it excludes, why it stays out of the chart), and `01-management-of-care/mandatory-reporting-categories.md` covers external duty-to-report categories driven by abuse, public-health, and forensic statutes. This Concept is the routing matrix in the middle: per-event, what form, where internal, whether external is also required, and the operative timeframe for each.

## Notes

- **Cell convention:** each `<br>`-separated item is one atomic Fact. No parentheticals in cells. Where a Fact appears with a qualifying clause (e.g., "Patient Safety Organization (if facility participates)"), the parenthetical is a scope condition, not bundled sub-Facts — the underlying Fact is still atomic; the qualifier disambiguates which facilities the route applies to. Same exception convention as the sibling `incident-report-rules.md`.
- **Atomicity flag — `Needlestick / sharps injury`.** The slash in the Row name is a canonical-pair exemption, not a comma-list. OSHA, CDC, and NIOSH frame "needlestick and sharps injury" as a single event category in 29 CFR 1910.1030(h)(5) (sharps injury log) and CDC's prevention literature; splitting into two Rows would create two Rows with identical Column values. Kept as one Row per the spec's atomicity flag.
- **Concept boundary.** This Concept covers the *routing matrix* — which form, where internally, whether externally, on what clock. The mechanics of what goes inside the incident report itself live in `01-management-of-care/incident-report-rules.md` (1.14/1.15). The external duty-to-report categories driven by abuse / public-health / forensic statutes live in `01-management-of-care/mandatory-reporting-categories.md` (1.14). NCLEX 2.6 questions test the routing layer here; 1.14 questions test the upstream content and downstream legal duty.
- **Why "Risk management" recurs across rows.** The internal-recipient column converges on Risk management for almost every category because the incident report is the universal internal artifact. The downstream divergence (Pharmacy for med errors, Employee health for needlesticks, Biomedical engineering for equipment, Security for violence) is what differentiates the rows for engine purposes — the engine can probe shared values via cross-row select-all on Risk management.
- **Why "Before end of shift" recurs.** Same widely-taught nursing-pedagogy framing flagged in `incident-report-rules.md` — no canonical AHRQ/CMS/TJC source mandates a specific shift-bound deadline. Listed in the unsourced-values table for SME confirmation; a sourced alternative would be "as soon as possible after the event" per AHRQ PSNet [s11].
- **Sentinel-event overlay.** Any of the six event categories can rise to sentinel-event status if the outcome is death, severe harm, or permanent harm reaching the patient [s10]. The 45-business-day TJC RCA clock applies when the facility chooses to report (TJC reporting is voluntary but expected for accredited facilities); the clock starts at organizational awareness. Sentinel-event examples and the full TJC list live in `01-management-of-care/sentinel-event-examples.md` (1.15).
- **OSHA 300 Log vs OSHA notification — different timeframes.** The OSHA 300 Log is internal recordkeeping (within 7 calendar days per 29 CFR 1904.7 [s1]) for any recordable work-related injury. OSHA *notification* (8 hours for fatality, 24 hours for hospitalization / amputation / eye loss per 29 CFR 1904.39 [s3]) is a separate external reporting duty triggered only by severe outcomes. Both can apply to the same needlestick or workplace-violence injury — they are not alternatives. The cells distinguish "OSHA 300 Log entry" (internal log, the form) from the timed external notifications (external reporting required).
- **Equipment malfunction — FDA MDR is mandatory for user facilities.** 21 CFR 803.30 sets a 10-working-day clock from awareness for both deaths and serious injuries caused by or contributed to by a medical device [s4]. Death reports go to FDA *and* the manufacturer; serious-injury reports go to the manufacturer (and to FDA if the manufacturer is unknown). MedWatch Form 3500A is the user-facility form. Distinguish from the voluntary MedWatch 3500 used by individual healthcare providers — that form is voluntary, the user-facility 3500A is mandatory.
- **Workplace violence external pathway.** OSHA does not have a violence-specific standard; workplace violence injuries are recordable / reportable to OSHA only via the general injury-recording rules (1904.7, 1904.8 for needlesticks, 1904.39 for severe-injury notification). NIOSH 2002-101 frames the post-incident response as employer-defined incident-reporting plus counseling; specific internal-recipient routing (supervisor / security / employee health) is the consensus framework but not a uniform federal mandate [s8]. Local-law-enforcement reporting is per facility policy and state law, which is why it appears with a "per facility policy" scope qualifier.
- **Tag note.** Carrying `IP-CD` (Communication and Documentation) — every cell in this Concept is fundamentally about a documentation artifact (which form) and a communication act (to whom). The Topic 2 README spec listed only `2.6, 1.14`; `IP-CD` added per the Phase 2b Tag-flexibility allowance documented in the skill file. `IP-CJ` was considered (the routing decision is a clinical-judgment call) but `IP-CD` is the dominant frame for this Concept's content.
- **Cross-Concept links.** Sibling `01-management-of-care/incident-report-rules.md` covers the internal-incident-report content and what NOT to include. Sibling `01-management-of-care/mandatory-reporting-categories.md` covers external duty-to-report categories (abuse, communicable disease, gunshot wound, impaired colleague). Sibling `01-management-of-care/sentinel-event-examples.md` covers the TJC sentinel-event taxonomy. Out of scope here: post-event root cause analysis content, OSHA Form 301 detail, full Joint Commission Sentinel Event Database submission protocol.

### Tricky distractors

- **OSHA 300 Log vs OSHA external notification.** Both apply to the same severe needlestick or violence injury. Wrong-answer pattern: "Recording on the OSHA 300 Log within 7 days satisfies OSHA reporting." Wrong if the injury also caused inpatient hospitalization, amputation, eye loss, or fatality — those trigger a separate 8-hour or 24-hour external notification.
- **FDA MedWatch — voluntary vs mandatory.** Form 3500 (provider) is voluntary; Form 3500A (user facility) is mandatory under 21 CFR 803.30 with a 10-working-day clock. Wrong-answer pattern: "Reporting to FDA is voluntary for hospitals." Wrong — a user facility (hospital) has a mandatory reporting duty when a device caused or contributed to a death or serious injury.
- **Equipment malfunction routing — manufacturer first for serious injury.** 21 CFR 803.30 routes serious-injury reports to the manufacturer (with FDA as fallback only if manufacturer is unknown), but routes death reports to both FDA and manufacturer. Wrong-answer pattern: "Report all equipment-related serious injuries to FDA within 10 days." Wrong — that's the death route; serious injury goes to manufacturer.
- **Sharps injury log vs OSHA 300 Log — both are required.** 29 CFR 1910.1030(h)(5) requires a separate sharps injury log (with type/brand of device, work area, and incident description); the OSHA 300 Log entry under 1904.8 is a separate recording on the general injury log. Wrong-answer pattern: "The OSHA 300 Log entry is sufficient." Wrong — both are required for a contaminated-sharps injury.
- **Needlestick post-exposure timeframe.** The needlestick should be reported as soon as possible after exposure to enable timely post-exposure prophylaxis (HIV PEP is most effective within hours, hepatitis evaluation within 24 hours). Wrong-answer pattern: "Complete the incident report at end of shift" — that's the form-completion convention, but the *exposure-to-evaluation* clock is "as soon as possible," not end-of-shift.
- **Workplace violence — fatality is 8 hours, not 24.** OSHA 1904.39 requires fatality notification within 8 hours; inpatient hospitalization, amputation, and eye loss are 24 hours. Wrong-answer pattern: "Notify OSHA within 24 hours of an employee fatality from workplace violence." Wrong — 8 hours.

## Sources

- `[s1]`: 29 CFR 1904.7, "General recording criteria," OSHA Recording and Reporting Occupational Injuries and Illnesses (retrieved 2026-04-26, https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/29/1904.7). Defines the recordable-injury criteria for the OSHA 300 Log.
- `[s2]`: 29 CFR 1904.8, "Recording criteria for needlestick and sharps injuries," OSHA Recording and Reporting Occupational Injuries and Illnesses (retrieved 2026-04-26, https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/29/1904.8). Mandates OSHA 300 Log recording for any work-related needlestick or sharps cut contaminated with blood or other potentially infectious material; requires privacy-case treatment (no employee name on the log). Cross-references 29 CFR 1910.1030 for the sharps injury log requirement.
- `[s3]`: 29 CFR 1904.39, "Reporting fatalities, hospitalizations, amputations, and losses of an eye as a result of work-related incidents to OSHA" (retrieved 2026-04-26, https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/29/1904.39). 8-hour fatality notification, 24-hour notification for inpatient hospitalization / amputation / loss of eye.
- `[s4]`: 21 CFR 803.30, "If I am a user facility, what reporting requirements apply to me?" FDA Medical Device Reporting (retrieved 2026-04-26, https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/21/803.30). 10-working-day clock; death reports route to FDA and manufacturer; serious-injury reports route to manufacturer (FDA fallback if manufacturer unknown); MedWatch Form 3500A is the user-facility form.

FDA MedWatch as the voluntary external reporting pathway for adverse drug events; pharmacy as a routing endpoint for medication-error events.
- `[s7]`: AHRQ PSNet, "Falls" primer (retrieved 2026-04-26, https://psnet.ahrq.gov/primer/falls). CMS hospital-acquired-condition non-payment for falls with injury; falls with injury classified as a never event; National Patient Safety Goal designation.
- `[s8]`: NIOSH, "Violence Occupational Hazards in Hospitals," Publication No. 2002-101 (retrieved 2026-04-26, https://www.cdc.gov/niosh/docs/2002-101/default.html).
