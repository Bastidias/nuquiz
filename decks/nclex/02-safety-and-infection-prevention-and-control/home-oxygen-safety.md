# Home Oxygen Safety

**Topic:** 2 — Safety and Infection Prevention and Control &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Pattern:** Aspects &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Tags:** 2.5, IP-TL
**Status:** draft (SME review pending)

Home oxygen safety is the bundle of patient and family teaching that reduces fire, burn, and equipment-failure risk when supplemental oxygen is delivered in the home. Oxygen is a medication that supports combustion: a small spark in an oxygen-enriched environment becomes a flash fire, and smoking is the dominant ignition source in oxygen-related home burns. NCSBN sub-objective 2.5 (Home Safety) tests this teaching as a Teaching/Learning artifact — what rules the nurse must convey before discharge so the client can use the equipment safely. The canonical primary sources are the open-access Open RN Nursing Skills textbook table on Oxygen Therapy Safety, the StatPearls Home Oxygen Therapy chapter, and PubMed Central peer-reviewed reviews of home-oxygen domestic fires.

## Notes

- **Cell convention:** each `<br>`-separated item is one atomic Fact. No parentheticals in cells.
- **Aspects single-Row scope qualifiers.** The "5 feet" distance qualifier in the "distance from open flame" Column and the "upright" qualifier in "storage" are inline because this is a single-Row Aspects Concept — splitting them into separate Columns would create Columns carrying only one cell each. Per `knowledge-map.md` Aspects parens-safety carve-out. No actual parentheses appear in any cell.
- **Five-foot rule sourcing.** The 5-foot separation between oxygen and any heat source / open flame / electrical device is the convention reflected in Open RN Nursing Skills Table 11.3b and the StatPearls Home Oxygen Therapy chapter. It originates in NFPA fire-code guidance for medical oxygen, which Open RN and StatPearls translate into the patient-teaching version cited here. NFPA itself is not on the NCLEX skill source allowlist; the citation chain runs through the NIH/NLM-hosted nursing textbook and StatPearls.
- **Smoking is the dominant ignition source.** PMC peer-reviewed reviews report that smoking provides the ignition in roughly 73–83% of medical-oxygen burn injuries, and 14–51% of home-oxygen patients continue smoking despite the contraindication. The exam-relevant teaching is that smoking by the patient OR by anyone else in the same room is unsafe — a household member smoking across the room is still a fire risk because oxygen leaks from the cannula and saturates clothing, hair, and beard hair.
- **Why no electric razors or hair dryers while wearing the cannula.** Both produce sparks and heat near the face, where leaked oxygen concentrates. The teaching is to remove the cannula for grooming or use battery-operated alternatives — but the canonical Open RN table phrases the rule as a no-use rule, which is what a wrong-answer-detecting question needs.
- **Petroleum-based lip balm.** Petroleum products on the lips or nares are flammable in an oxygen-enriched environment and can also melt onto the cannula. Water-based lubricants (e.g., water-based gels) are the substitution. This is the most-tested NCLEX detail in this Concept.
- **CMS regulatory framing is for context, not for cell content.** CMS LCD L33797 establishes Medicare coverage criteria for home oxygen (qualifying ABG/SpO2 thresholds, supplier requirements). It does not enumerate patient-teaching safety rules — those come from the clinical sources. Cited here for the supplier-contact and prescription-with-the-patient transport Fact only.
- **Cross-Concept links.** Sibling `home-safety-by-population.md` (2.5) covers age-specific home hazards (infant suffocation, adolescent firearm access, older-adult fall risks). Sibling `fire-response-race-pass.md` (2.2) covers facility fire response — this Concept covers prevention in the home. Sibling `oxygen-delivery-devices.md` (T8, future) will cover the device taxonomy (nasal cannula vs simple mask vs Venturi vs non-rebreather) — this Concept covers the safety wrap-around regardless of device. Out of scope here: the clinical decision of when to start home oxygen (LCD ABG/SpO2 thresholds), titration to target SpO2, and pulmonary rehabilitation (all Topic 7 / Topic 8).
- **NCSBN 2.5 framing.** Sub-objective 2.5 (Home Safety) is explicitly a Teaching/Learning surface — the nurse's job is to deliver and confirm understanding of these rules before discharge. Tag `IP-TL` is therefore primary.

### Tricky distractors

- **Distance from flame: 5 feet vs 3 feet vs 10 feet.** Five feet is the canonical teaching. NCLEX items routinely seed wrong-answer distractors at 3 feet (too close) and 10 feet (correct in some hospital-engineering contexts but not the patient-teaching number).
- **Petroleum-based lip balm vs water-based lip balm.** The wrong-answer pattern is "apply Vaseline to chapped lips before sleep." The right answer is a water-based lubricant. This is one of the highest-yield home-oxygen distractors.
- **Smoking by household member vs only by patient.** Many wrong answers limit the no-smoking rule to the patient alone. The teaching is that no one smokes in the same room as the oxygen equipment, regardless of whether the patient is wearing the cannula at that moment.
- **Cylinder on the client's bed during transport.** A common bedside-convenience action that is explicitly contraindicated. The cylinder must be in a holder, cart, or sling — never on the bed.
- **Storage of full vs partial cylinders together.** Mixed storage is the wrong answer; segregated storage is right. The rationale (full cylinders deliver more oxygen at higher pressure to a fire if they vent) is exam-out-of-scope but anchors the rule.
- **Electric razor / hair dryer while wearing the cannula.** Wrong-answer distractor: "use a hair dryer on cool setting." Heat setting is irrelevant — the spark from the motor is the hazard. The rule is no use while wearing the cannula, regardless of setting.
