# Sterile Field Maintenance Rules

**Topic:** 2 — Safety and Infection Prevention and Control &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Pattern:** Aspects &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Tags:** 2.9, IP-CJ
**Status:** draft (SME review pending)

Once a sterile field has been established for an invasive procedure, a small set of geometric and behavioral rules govern whether it stays sterile. NCLEX-RN tests these rules under sub-objective 2.9 — given a clinical scenario, the nurse must recognize when sterility has been broken and act on the breach. This Concept inventories the five rule families that appear repeatedly in NCSBN-style scenarios: the 1-inch border (where the field's edge becomes contaminated), the above-waist rule (the vertical zone in which the field stays sterile), the line-of-sight rule (the field is sterile only while it's monitored), the moisture rule (water bridges sterility from the unsterile side to the sterile side), and the motion rule (movement, traffic, and reaching all create contamination risk).

**Layout convention:** single Row (sterile field). Columns are the five rule families, ordered left → right by the dimension they govern: edge geometry (1-inch border), vertical geometry (above-waist), supervision (line-of-sight), physical state (moisture), kinetic behavior (motion). Each `<br>`-separated item is one atomic Fact. ## Notes

- **Cell convention:** each `<br>`-separated item is one atomic Fact. No parentheticals in cells (Aspects parens-safety carve-out applies, but no carve-out was needed in this draft — every Fact is unqualified).
- **Pattern choice — Aspects.** A sterile field is one thing with multiple facets (edge, height, supervision, moisture, motion). It is not a comparison of entities (Dimensions) and not a sequence of steps (Ordered). Aspects is the right Pattern.
- **Why 1 inch.** The 1-inch border is a perioperative-nursing convention codified in AORN's Guideline for Sterile Technique and reproduced in StatPearls. It is not a measurement of microbial migration distance — it is a margin of safety the field is set up to respect when items are tossed, and it widens to include any visible hole, tear, or damp area.
- **Aseptic non-touch technique vs sterile field maintenance.** This Concept is about maintaining the field once established. Sibling `surgical-vs-medical-asepsis.md` (2.9) covers when sterile technique is required vs clean technique; sibling `ppe-donning-doffing-sequence.md` (2.9) covers donning order before approaching the field; sibling `aseptic-technique-medication-admin.md` (2.9 / 6.6 / 6.7) covers aseptic non-touch technique during medication administration (different from full surgical asepsis).
- **Strikethrough.** "Strikethrough" is the perioperative term for fluid wicking through a permeable barrier from an unsterile side to a sterile side, contaminating the sterile side. The moisture-rule Facts above capture this without using the term as a Fact (it would be vocabulary, not a rule). Notes carries it.
- **Maximal sterile barrier vs sterile field maintenance.** Maximal sterile barrier (mask + cap + gown + sterile gloves + full-body drape) is a CDC/HICPAC-elevated requirement specifically for central-line insertion. It governs PPE composition, not field-maintenance behavior; sibling `surgical-vs-medical-asepsis.md` carries that Fact.
- **Out of scope.** This Concept does NOT cover: PPE donning/doffing order; surgical hand antisepsis duration or technique; specific contamination response per procedure type (e.g., discard contaminated catheter and obtain a new sterile one — that's the Breach response column in `surgical-vs-medical-asepsis.md`); when surgical asepsis is required vs medical asepsis (sibling Concept).

### Tricky distractors

- **Edge contamination vs surface contamination.** The 1-inch border is contaminated even though the rest of the table-top field is sterile. Wrong-answer pattern: a question asks whether a sterile item placed *on the field* near the edge is sterile; students who learn "the field is sterile" without learning "the 1-inch border is unsterile" miss this.
- **Above-table vs above-waist vs above-shoulder.** Three different vertical references. Above table top = sterile field plane. Below waist = contaminated zone. Above shoulder = contaminated zone (operator's arms can drift outside line of sight). Wrong-answer pattern: collapsing the three into one rule ("anything above the table is sterile") and missing that raised hands can leave the sterile zone too.
- **Covered vs unattended sterile field.** A sterile field can be covered with a sterile drape and remain sterile under specific conditions (sign / limited traffic / direct observation). An unattended field — turned away from, walked away from — is contaminated. Wrong-answer pattern: assuming any sterile field that is not actively in use is contaminated (over-strict) or that a covered field needs no monitoring (under-strict).
- **Moisture from above vs moisture from below.** Liquid spilled onto a sterile drape from a sterile container generally remains sterile. Liquid wicking up from an unsterile surface beneath the drape is strikethrough — contamination. Wrong-answer pattern: treating all moisture identically.
- **Reaching across vs reaching around.** Reaching across an open sterile field with an unsterile arm contaminates the field below. Reaching around the field (with the arm staying outside the field's airspace) does not. NCLEX scenarios test this distinction in pack-opening questions.
