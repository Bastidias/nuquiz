# Isolation Precaution Types

**Topic:** 2 — Safety and Infection Prevention and Control &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Pattern:** Dimensions &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Tags:** 2.9, IP-CJ, IP-CD
**Status:** draft (SME review pending)

CDC Guideline for Isolation Precautions in Healthcare Settings (HICPAC, 2007 with September 2024 updates) defines a tiered system: Standard Precautions for every patient encounter, Transmission-Based Precautions (Contact, Droplet, Airborne) layered on top when a pathogen's transmission route warrants more than Standard alone, and a Protective Environment for allogeneic HSCT patients to defend a severely immunocompromised host from environmental fungi. Some pathogens require two layered Transmission-Based categories (e.g., Contact + Droplet for SARS-CoV-2 and pandemic influenza in current CDC guidance; Contact + Airborne for varicella, smallpox, disseminated zoster, and SARS) — those combinations are their own canonical Rows because PPE, room, and transport rules sum the requirements of both parents. The "+" in Row labels is a deliberate additive operator, not a parens-style sub-attribute. `Protective/Reverse` is the slash exemption: the two terms name the same precaution category (CDC's canonical name is "Protective Environment"; "reverse isolation" is the older clinical synonym still in heavy NCLEX use). Columns are the five attributes a nurse must operationalize at the bedside: example pathogens, room requirement, PPE, transport rule, duration.

## Notes

- **Cell convention:** each `<br>`-separated item is one atomic Fact. No parentheticals in cells.
- **Why Standard Precautions is its own Row, not a column qualifier.** Standard Precautions are applied to every patient at all times; Transmission-Based Precautions are layered on top when a pathogen's route is not interrupted by Standard alone [s1]. Modeling Standard as a sibling Row to Contact / Droplet / Airborne preserves the comparison the engine wants to test (which precaution is added when the patient has X?), and the `duration` cell ("Applied to every patient encounter at all times") makes the always-on framing explicit.
- **Why combination Rows (`Contact + droplet`, `Contact + airborne`) earn their own slots.** PPE and room rules for combinations are the union of the parent categories, not a third thing. Splitting into separate Rows lets the engine detect that, for example, varicella and smallpox share `Airborne infection isolation room (AIIR)` with TB and measles AND share `Gown` with C. difficile and MRSA — visibility that collapses if combinations are stuffed into a parenthetical on one of the parent Rows. The "+" operator in the Row label is meaningful: it signals additive layering of two parent precaution categories.
- **Why `Protective / Reverse` carries a slash.** CDC's canonical term is "Protective Environment" (PE) [s1]; "reverse isolation" is the older clinical synonym still encountered in NCLEX stems and bedside vocabulary. The slash exemption (canonical-name aliasing) is documented in `knowledge-map.md` § Atomicity. The Row covers what CDC defines and validates only for allogeneic HSCT recipients; pedagogical extension to other severely neutropenic populations is widely taught but not in CDC's formal scope, so the `example pathogens` cell stays narrow.
- **Standard Precautions PPE is exposure-driven.** Unlike the Transmission-Based categories, Standard PPE is determined by the anticipated exposure during the specific task, not by the patient's diagnosis [s1]. Venipuncture may need only gloves; intubation needs gloves + gown + mask + eye protection.
- **Cohorting.** When single-patient rooms are unavailable, CDC permits cohorting patients infected with the same organism for Contact and Droplet Precautions [s1]. Cohorting is NOT acceptable for Airborne Precautions across different airborne pathogens (e.g., a TB patient and a measles patient cannot share an AIIR).
- **AIIR specifications are facility-level, not nurse-level.** The 12 air exchanges, negative pressure, and HEPA filtration are engineering controls a unit either has or does not [s1]. The nurse's bedside duties under Airborne Precautions are: confirm the door is closed, don a fit-tested N95 before entry, and ensure the patient masks during any necessary transport.
- **C. difficile hand hygiene exception.** Soap and water is preferred over alcohol-based handrub when caring for C. difficile patients because alcohol does not kill C. difficile spores [s2]. Alcohol-based handrub remains acceptable for the standard hand-hygiene moments with non-C. difficile patients on Contact Precautions.
- **Norovirus mask exception.** Personnel cleaning areas heavily contaminated with feces or vomitus from norovirus patients may wear a mask because the virus can be aerosolized from those substances during cleaning [s2]. This is an environmental-cleaning-task exception under Contact Precautions, not a category change to Droplet.
- **Pertussis duration trap.** Pertussis Droplet Precautions end 5 days after initiation of effective antibiotic therapy [s2], not after symptom resolution and not after 24 hours. The 24-hour rule applies to N. meningitidis, group A streptococcus, and H. influenzae type b — distinct organisms, distinct duration cell.
- **TB skin-test positive without active disease.** A patient with a positive PPD or IGRA but no signs of active pulmonary disease requires only Standard Precautions [s2]. Wrong-answer pattern: putting a latent-TB patient in an AIIR.
- **Out of scope for this Concept.** PPE donning and doffing sequence (sibling: `ppe-donning-doffing-sequence.md`). Hand-hygiene 5 moments (sibling: `hand-hygiene-five-moments.md`). Sterile field maintenance and surgical asepsis (siblings: `sterile-field-maintenance.md`, `surgical-vs-medical-asepsis.md`). Pathogen-by-pathogen lookup including expanded gastroenteritis and viral hemorrhagic fever rows (sibling: `transmission-based-precaution-pathogens.md`). Chain of infection (sibling: `chain-of-infection.md`).

### Tricky distractors

- **Droplet vs Airborne.** Droplet pathogens travel only short distances (≤3 feet) on respiratory droplets and require only a surgical mask in a single-patient room without special air handling [s1]. Airborne pathogens stay aloft on droplet nuclei over long distances and require an AIIR plus a fit-tested N95 [s1]. Wrong-answer pattern: assigning an N95 to a meningococcal patient (Droplet, surgical mask suffices) or assigning a surgical mask to a TB patient (Airborne, N95 required).
- **Varicella vs measles vs TB.** All three are airborne. Varicella also requires Contact Precautions because of the vesicular skin lesions, making varicella a Contact + Airborne combination [s2]. Measles and TB are Airborne only. Wrong-answer pattern: omitting the gown and gloves for a varicella patient.
- **Pandemic / novel respiratory pathogens.** SARS-CoV-2 and pandemic influenza are managed under Contact + Droplet with the addition of N95 and AIIR for aerosol-generating procedures per current CDC guidance [s3]. The 2007 guideline's original SARS row classifies SARS as Contact + Droplet + Airborne [s2]; current pandemic guidance for SARS-CoV-2 leans Contact + Droplet with respirator-during-AGPs framing. NCLEX answers should track current CDC guidance, not the 2007 baseline.
- **Localized vs disseminated zoster.** Localized herpes zoster in an immunocompetent patient requires only Standard Precautions with the lesions covered [s2]. Disseminated zoster, OR localized zoster in an immunocompromised patient, escalates to Contact + Airborne [s2]. Wrong-answer pattern: putting every shingles patient in an AIIR.
- **Protective Environment is positive pressure; AIIR is negative pressure.** Protective Environment defends the patient by pushing filtered air OUT of the room (positive pressure relative to corridor) [s1]. AIIR defends others by pulling contaminated air IN to the room (negative pressure relative to corridor) [s1]. A patient who is BOTH severely immunocompromised AND has an airborne infection requires an anteroom or portable HEPA arrangement to satisfy both [s1].
- **MRSA pneumonia is Contact, not Droplet.** MRSA's primary route is contact with skin or contaminated surfaces and equipment, not respiratory droplets [s1]. The mistake is assuming "pneumonia" means Droplet — the precaution category tracks the organism's transmission route, not the body site.
- **Cohort restriction for Airborne.** Airborne patients with different pathogens cannot share an AIIR even though both are airborne. Each gets a dedicated AIIR.

- `Airborne | room requirement → ?` → Airborne infection isolation room (AIIR), Single-patient room with monitored negative pressure relative to the surrounding area, 12 air exchanges per hour for new construction or renovation, 6 air exchanges per hour for existing facilities, Air exhausted directly to the outside or recirculated through HEPA filtration, Door kept closed (multi-Fact cell)
- `Droplet | PPE → ?` → Surgical mask for close contact with infectious patient, Don mask upon room entry, Respirator not required (multi-Fact cell)

- `? | room requirement → Airborne infection isolation room (AIIR)` → Airborne, Contact + airborne (cross-Row select-all, isolates the AIIR-requiring categories)
- `? | PPE → Gown` → Contact, Contact + droplet, Contact + airborne (cross-Row select-all on the gown-requiring categories)
- `Contact + airborne | example pathogens → ?` → Varicella (chickenpox), Disseminated herpes zoster, Localized herpes zoster in immunocompromised patient until disseminated infection ruled out, Smallpox, SARS
- `Protective / Reverse | room requirement → ?` → Single-patient room with positive room air pressure relative to the corridor, Pressure differential greater than 2.5 Pa, HEPA filtration of incoming air at 99.97% efficiency for particles 0.3 micrometers and larger, Greater than 12 air changes per hour, Well-sealed room with self-closing door, Directed room air flow from supply across patient bed to exhaust on opposite side, No carpeting in patient room, No upholstered furniture, No fresh or dried flowers or potted plants (multi-Fact cell — exposes the positive-pressure-vs-negative-pressure distinction with Airborne)
- Composite Row profile: Airborne, with `room requirement` swapped to "Single-patient room with positive room air pressure relative to the corridor" — tests AIIR-vs-PE pressure-direction discrimination.
- Composite Row profile: Droplet, with `PPE` swapped to "Fit-tested NIOSH-certified N95 or higher respirator donned prior to room entry" — tests droplet-vs-airborne mask discrimination (e.g., wrong-answer pattern for meningococcal patient).
