# Aseptic Technique for Medication Administration

**Topic:** 2 — Safety and Infection Prevention and Control &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Pattern:** Aspects &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Tags:** 2.9, 6.6, 6.7, IP-CJ
**Status:** draft (SME review pending)

Aseptic technique for medication administration is the bundle of micro-rules a nurse follows when drawing up and delivering an injectable or infused drug so that no pathogen is introduced into the medication, the IV system, or the patient. It is a single artifact with multiple facets — when hand hygiene is performed, how the vial or ampule is accessed, how the IV port is disinfected before each access, what rules govern multi-dose vials, and what is done when sterility is broken. The CDC Safe Injection Practices guidance (One & Only Campaign), CDC's intravascular catheter recommendations, and the open-access NCBI Nursing Advanced Skills chapter on IV push medications are the canonical primary sources. NCLEX-RN tests this primarily under sub-objective 2.9 (Surgical Asepsis) for the technique itself and sub-objectives 6.6 and 6.7 for the medication-administration and parenteral-therapies application.

Filed in Topic 2 because the aseptic technique itself (hand hygiene timing, vial/ampule rules, port disinfection) is the load-bearing content; Topic 6 sibling `injection-technique-by-route.md` references this Concept for the hygiene component rather than duplicating it.

## Notes

- **Cell convention:** each `<br>`-separated item is one atomic Fact. No parentheticals in cells.
- **Aspects single-Row Pattern.** This Concept is one technique bundle with multiple facets — Aspects is correct here. A Dimensions framing with Rows = "preparation phase" / "access phase" / "post-event" was considered and rejected: the Columns of that framing would be ad-hoc Fact lists with no shared attribute structure across Rows, which is exactly the case Aspects exists for.
- **Column header `vial/ampule technique`.** The slash names two related techniques covered together (single-dose vials, multi-dose vials, and glass ampules all share the underlying "withdraw a sterile aliquot from a primary container" task), and the cell content addresses both technique types within atomic Facts. Per the column-header-qualifier carve-out in `knowledge-map.md`, the slash in the Column header scopes the Column rather than smuggling Facts into a cell.
- **Scrub time — the 5-second floor vs longer scrubs.** The NCBI Nursing Advanced Skills chapter cites at least 5 seconds of vigorous mechanical friction as the floor `[s2]`. CDC's intravascular guideline `[s3]` does not quantify duration; INS Standards and other facility protocols often specify 5–15 seconds. NCLEX answer convention follows the at-least-5-second floor with allowance for facility policy specifying longer. Listed as "at least 5 seconds" to preserve the floor.
- **Antiseptic choice for hub disinfection.** CDC §19.4 lists chlorhexidine, povidone-iodine, an iodophor, OR 70% alcohol as acceptable. The Nursing Advanced Skills chapter narrows operative practice to 70% alcohol or alcohol-based chlorhexidine. NCLEX has accepted any of the four antiseptics in answer keys; the Facts above carry all four for engine flexibility.
- **Filter-needle direction-of-flow rule.** A filter needle is used to withdraw medication from an ampule (catches glass) and is replaced before injection (the filter is on the wrong side for delivery). The reverse — using a filter needle to inject — would push glass through the filter, which is not its design. Captured as two atomic Facts (withdraw with filter needle / replace before injection) so the engine can probe each.
- **Ampule never recapped vs needle never recapped.** The recapping rule (do not recap a used needle except by single-handed scoop technique) is a sharps-safety Fact, NOT an aseptic-technique Fact. Out of scope here — covered in Topic 2 sibling `hazardous-material-categories.md` and Topic 6 sibling `injection-technique-by-route.md`.
- **Hand hygiene before glove donning.** CDC explicitly requires hand hygiene before donning sterile or non-sterile gloves and immediately after glove removal `[s2, s4]`. Donning gloves does not substitute for hand hygiene — this is a high-yield NCLEX distractor pair.
- **Cross-Concept links.** Sibling `hand-hygiene-five-moments.md` (2.9) covers the WHO 5-moments framework; this Concept zooms in on the medication-administration application of Moments 2 and 3. Sibling `surgical-vs-medical-asepsis.md` (2.9) covers the broader sterile-vs-clean-technique distinction; this Concept covers the medication-administration micro-rules within both postures (medication preparation is medical asepsis with ANTT; central-line access is surgical asepsis with maximal barrier). Sibling `sterile-field-maintenance.md` (2.9) covers the geometry of the sterile field once established. Topic 6 sibling `injection-technique-by-route.md` (6.6) references this Concept for the hygiene component of injection technique.
- **Out of scope.** This Concept does NOT cover: route-specific injection technique (IM landmarks, SubQ angle, ID bleb formation — Topic 6); the Six/Ten Rights of medication administration (Topic 6 sibling `medication-administration-rights.md`); high-alert medication double-check rules (Topic 6); needle-recap and sharps-disposal rules (Topic 2 `hazardous-material-categories.md`); central-line dressing change technique beyond hub disinfection (Topic 7); and chemotherapy-specific aseptic and PPE rules (Topic 2 `chemotherapy-handling.md`).

### Tricky distractors

- **Filter needle to draw vs to inject.** A filter needle is used to draw medication from a glass ampule to catch any glass shards. Before injecting the patient, the nurse REPLACES the filter needle with a regular sterile needle. Wrong-answer pattern: "Inject the patient with the filter needle still attached" — this would push captured glass particles through the filter directly into the patient. Wrong-answer pattern: "Draw from the ampule with a regular needle" — risks aspirating glass.
- **Single-dose vial vs multi-dose vial reuse.** A single-dose vial is for one patient, one procedure, even if medication remains. A multi-dose vial may be used for repeated entries (preferably for one patient) for up to 28 days from opening. Wrong-answer pattern: "Save the leftover medication in the single-dose vial for the next patient" — wrong; pool/store of single-dose contents is explicitly prohibited.
- **Scrub-the-hub vs spike-and-go.** Every access of a needleless connector requires a fresh scrub — not just the first access of the shift. Wrong-answer pattern: "The nurse already scrubbed the port at the start of the shift, so the next IV push is fine without re-scrubbing." Wrong; each access requires its own scrub-and-dry sequence.
- **Hand hygiene before glove donning.** Gloves are not a substitute for hand hygiene; gloves can have micro-perforations and hands can contaminate the outside of the glove during donning. Wrong-answer pattern: "Don gloves, then perform hand hygiene over the gloves" — wrong; sequence is hand hygiene FIRST, then gloves.
- **Snap the ampule toward vs away from the hands.** Snapping the ampule neck away from the hands and body protects the nurse from glass shards and aerosolized medication. Wrong-answer pattern: "Snap the ampule neck toward the chest for control" — wrong; force vector should be away from the body.
- **Air injection direction for vial vs ampule.** Vial: inject air equal to the volume to be withdrawn (creates positive pressure, allows medication to flow out cleanly). Ampule: do NOT inject air — air injected into an ampule will displace medication out of the open neck. Wrong-answer pattern: "Inject air into the ampule before withdrawing." Wrong.
