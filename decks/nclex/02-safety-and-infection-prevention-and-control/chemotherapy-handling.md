# Chemotherapy Handling Precautions

**Topic:** 2 — Safety and Infection Prevention and Control &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Pattern:** Aspects &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Tags:** 2.4, 6-cross-tag, IP-CJ
**Status:** draft (SME review pending)

Chemotherapy and other antineoplastic agents are hazardous drugs that pose acute and chronic risks to nurses through skin contact, mucous-membrane contact, inhalation of aerosolized particles, ingestion, and needlestick. NCLEX 2.4 (Handling Hazardous and Infectious Materials) and Topic 6 high-alert-medication content together test the bedside nurse's responsibilities: PPE selection, route-specific safe-administration technique, spill-kit response, exposure-response sequence, and reproductive-risk accommodations. The federal/professional framework is USP <800> (referenced via PMC peer-reviewed literature) layered with NIOSH hazardous-drug guidance, ONS position statements, and the ASCO/ONS Antineoplastic Therapy Administration Safety Standards.

## Notes

- **Cell convention:** each `<br>`-separated item is one atomic Fact. No parentheticals in cells.
- **Aspects single-Row scope qualifiers.** Several Facts carry timing or scope qualifiers (e.g., "for 15 minutes," "every 30 minutes," "for 48 hours after administration"). These qualifiers are part of the canonical USP <800> / NIOSH / ONS phrasing — stripping them would change the Fact (the 15-minute flush rule is exam-tested specifically because the duration matters). Per `knowledge-map.md` Aspects parens-safety carve-out, splitting these into a separate Column would create Columns carrying only one cell. Kept inline as documented exception. No actual parentheses appear in cells.
- **Why this Concept is Aspects, not Dimensions.** The sibling `hazardous-material-categories.md` Concept already carries a `Chemotherapy waste` Row and a `Hazardous drug spill` Row across Dimensions Columns (container/disposal, PPE, spill protocol, regulatory standard). This Concept zooms in on the chemotherapy-specific facets at greater depth — it is one thing (chemotherapy handling) elaborated across multiple aspects, not multiple categories compared on shared attributes. Aspects is the correct Pattern.
- **USP <800> citation pathway.** USP itself is not on the project source allowlist (USP standards are paywalled and copyrighted). Per the user's guidance, USP <800> content is sourced through peer-reviewed open-access literature in PMC (PMC9900183, PMC4252184, PMC5057202, PMC5398627) that quotes and analyzes the standard. NIOSH, CDC, AHRQ, and ONS position statements provide the federal-floor and professional-organization framing.
- **Two-person verification is in scope, not in this Concept.** ASCO/ONS Antineoplastic Therapy Administration Safety Standards require independent double-checks of chemotherapy orders before administration [s7]. This is a high-alert-medication verification rule and lives in Topic 6's `high-alert-medications` and `medication-administration-rights` Concepts, not in the handling-precautions Aspects table here. Cross-referenced in the cross-Concept links below.
- **48-hour patient-excreta rule.** NIOSH guidance specifies that PPE is required when handling excreta from patients who have received hazardous drugs within the prior 48 hours; some sources extend this window for specific drugs with longer excretion half-lives. The 48-hour figure is the predominant teaching and the operative rule on NCLEX 2.4 items.
- **Tag note.** Carrying `IP-CJ` (Clinical Judgment) because route-specific PPE selection, exposure-response sequencing, and the pregnancy-accommodation request are recognize-cues / take-action / generate-solutions decisions the bedside nurse owns. The Topic 2 README spec was `2.4, 6-cross-tag`; per skill Tag-flexibility note, `IP-CJ` is added because the content is fundamentally a clinical-judgment workflow.
- **Cross-Concept links.** Sibling `hazardous-material-categories.md` (Topic 2) covers chemotherapy at the waste-stream / category level — what container, what disposal, what regulatory authority. This Concept covers the route-specific administration and exposure-response slice. Sibling `reportable-event-categories.md` (Topic 2) covers the post-event reporting flow when a chemotherapy spill or exposure occurs (incident report, OSHA injury log). Sibling `incident-report-rules.md` (Topic 1) covers the report mechanics. Topic 6 high-alert-medication Concepts (TBD) cover the verification and dosing rules; Topic 6 cross-Tag is the placeholder until that content is authored. Out of scope here: specific antineoplastic drug names and their NIOSH List-of-Hazardous-Drugs classifications (Topic 6 territory), antiemetic premedication regimens (Topic 6), extravasation management (Topic 7 territory).

### Tricky distractors

- **Surgical mask vs N95 for aerosol.** Wrong-answer pattern: "A standard surgical mask is sufficient when administering inhaled chemotherapy or aerosol-generating intravesical instillation." Wrong — NIOSH-certified N95 or N100 fit-tested respirator is required when aerosol risk is present [s1, s2]. Surgical masks are not respirators and do not provide a tested seal.
- **Single glove vs double glove.** Wrong-answer pattern: "One pair of nitrile gloves is sufficient for IV chemotherapy administration." Wrong — USP <800> mandates two pairs of chemotherapy-rated gloves tested to ASTM D6978 [s1, s2]. Single-gloving is a citation finding.
- **Eye flush duration.** Wrong-answer pattern: "Flush the eye with sterile saline for 5 minutes after splash exposure." Wrong — the operative rule is at least 15 minutes at an eyewash station [s1, s2]. The 15-minute figure is shared across the skin-wash and eye-flush rules and is heavily tested.
- **Crushing tablets at the bedside.** Wrong-answer pattern: "The nurse crushes the patient's oral chemotherapy tablet in a medicine cup and mixes it with applesauce for swallowing." Wrong — oral hazardous-drug tablets must be crushed inside a biological safety cabinet (typically pharmacy), not at the bedside [s1]. Bedside crushing aerosolizes the drug.
- **Pregnancy = involuntary reassignment.** Wrong-answer pattern: "A pregnant nurse must be removed from chemotherapy administration." Wrong framing — the convention is to OFFER alternative duty and to inform the staff member of reproductive hazards; the staff member's right is to request accommodation, and the institution must have a documented accommodation process [s1, s5, s6]. Mandatory reassignment is not the rule.
- **Patient excreta after chemotherapy.** Wrong-answer pattern: "Standard precautions are sufficient when emptying the urinal of a patient who received chemotherapy yesterday." Wrong — chemotherapy PPE (chemo gloves, gown, eye/face protection per splash risk) is required for handling excreta within 48 hours after administration [s2, s5].
- **Recapping a chemotherapy needle.** Wrong-answer pattern: "Recap the needle before disposing in the chemotherapy sharps container." Wrong — recapping is prohibited; the safety-engineered device is activated and the sharp is placed directly into the chemotherapy-rated sharps container without recapping. (See sibling `hazardous-material-categories.md` for the OSHA recapping rule.)
